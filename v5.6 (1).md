# ACT ONE: THE POINT OF NO RETURN



# Chapter 1


## Transit


### Month 3, Day 87 — July 2036

0.58 AU.
Cassian Vale ran the number the way he'd been trained to — twice, because you always check twice, because the instructors at Dryden had drilled it until the arithmetic was autonomic, something his hands did before his mind caught up. He'd washed out of flight school at nineteen. Mild astigmatism, slight depth perception variance. Not enough to matter for most things. Enough to matter for the one thing he'd wanted.
The habit had stayed. Seven years later, on a ship he'd never fly, he still counted distance in AU and velocity in delta-v and time in orbital windows. It was the language he had for where he was.
Some nights he ran the other calculations — not the cultivation numbers, not the nutrient cycling he could do asleep. Hohmann transfer windows. Insertion burns. Orbital corrections for a spacecraft that would never be his to correct. He did them the way some people reread letters from someone who isn't coming back.
0.58 AU to Mars. Ninety-two days if the trajectory held nominal. The ship's methalox tanks had burned through trans-Mars injection in Week 6, committing them to the long elliptical fall. No abort trajectories left. No turning back.
He stood at the cultivation lab viewport, coffee going cold in his hand, watching Mars — a copper point in the dark, not yet a disc, not yet a place. Behind him the hydroponics pumps kept their steady rhythm. The LED grow lights hummed at a frequency he'd stopped hearing months ago.
"Talking to the void again?" Thea Miren appeared beside him, her reflection ghosting across the viewport. Her dark hair was pulled back in a practical bun, and she held two squeeze bottles of what passed for coffee aboard the Prometheus. "I brought stimulants."
Cassian accepted the bottle with a half-smile. "Just doing the math. We're at 0.58 AU from Mars. Another 92 days if everything stays nominal."

Thea didn't answer immediately. She looked at the viewport, where Mars hung like a copper coin in the dark. "0.58 AU," she repeated quietly. "You know most people just say three months."

"Most people weren't—" He stopped.

She waited.

"Most people count it differently."

She looked at him for a moment, then let it go.

"Everything won't stay nominal." Thea sipped her coffee, grimacing at the taste. "ARIA flagged three equipment alerts this morning. Nothing critical, but the trend line isn't encouraging."
"Let me guess. The water recycler?"
"And the primary CO₂ scrubber. Marcus is working on it." She paused, her reflection's eyes meeting his in the glass. "You know, the Ares II crew made it this far before their scrubber failed on the return. That's what killed them."
Cassian didn't answer. He didn't need to. Every crew member on this ship had studied the same mission files, watched the same footage of the two survivors being carried off the return vehicle — hollow-eyed, unable to stand in Earth gravity, clutching a sample case that held five kilograms of organism and the knowledge that six of their crewmates were still floating in a dead ship somewhere beyond Mars orbit.
Five kilograms. Enough to prove Areserine worked. Enough to treat 2,500 patients. Not enough to save the other five hundred million.
That's why there were twenty-four of them now, instead of eight.
"Cassian?" Thea was watching him. "Where'd you go?"
"Emily." The name came out before he could stop it. "Just thinking about Emily."
Her expression softened. She knew about his sister — everyone did. Cassian's sister is Stage 2 Genome Collapse Syndrome. He's here to save her. It had been part of the crew dossiers Mei Tanaka distributed during training, each person's reason reduced to a sentence. Out here, 92 days from Mars and 160 million kilometers from Earth, the sentence felt like a prayer that had stopped working.
"Have you gotten a message from her lately?"
"Three days ago. She said she's stable. The rationed Areserine from Ares II is keeping her in stasis, but..." He trailed off. They both knew the math there, too. Five kilograms had treated 2,500 patients for roughly two years each. Emily had been lucky enough to win the lottery for treatment. Five hundred million others hadn't been.
The silence stretched between them, filled only by the ship's ambient hum. Somewhere in the habitat ring, someone laughed—probably Jackson Moore, whose voice carried through the ventilation system like a foghorn. Farther away, the steady clanging of tools on metal suggested Marcus Okafor was deep in repair work on the CO₂ scrubber.
"You should eat something," Thea said finally. "Chef Cassian hasn't made an appearance in the galley for three days. People are starting to notice."
"I'm not a chef."
"You're the closest thing we have. Your spirulina pasta almost tastes like food." She squeezed his shoulder, her hand warm through the thin fabric of his jumpsuit. "Come on. I'll help you cook. People are getting weird about meals and I think it's because the food is depressing."
"The food *is* depressing."
"Right. So fix it. That's what you do."
Despite himself, Cassian smiled. Thea had that effect on him — had since the first week of training, when she'd beaten him at chess three times in a row and then offered to teach him how to lose with dignity.
"Fine. But you're chopping the mushrooms."
"Deal."
They left the viewport behind, Mars continuing its slow expansion in the frame.
In the corridor, Thea's voice dropped. "Have you noticed Rachel lately?"
"Park? Not really. Why?"
"She's been quiet. Quieter than usual, I mean. Skipping meals. I tried to talk to her yesterday and she just... smiled. Said everything was fine. But her eyes..." Thea shook her head. "I don't know. Something felt off."
"Should we tell Mei?"
"I mentioned it. She said she'd check in." Thea's jaw tightened. "But Mei's been off too. Everyone's been off. Three months in a tin can will do that."
They reached the galley — a cramped space with a heating unit, a prep counter, and storage lockers. They made spirulina noodles with mushroom sauce, the mushrooms pale and rubbery from the ship's agricultural bay. They ate standing at the counter.
Jackson Moore stepped through the doorway, his bulky frame filling the entrance. The mining technician had a smile that never quite reached his eyes and a voice that carried whether you wanted it to or not.
"Smells good in here." Jackson moved to the pot without asking, serving himself a generous portion. "Chef Cassian strikes again."
"Help yourself," Cassian said dryly.
"Already am." Jackson took a bite, chewing thoughtfully. "Not bad. Better than the slop they serve at standard mess hours." He glanced at Thea. "You know, some of us have been talking. About maybe setting up a private arrangement. People who appreciate good food contributing a little extra—some ration credits, maybe some work trade—in exchange for meals like this."
Thea's expression cooled. "You're talking about a black market."
"I'm talking about incentive structures." Jackson's smile widened. "Everyone has something they want. Everyone has something they can trade. That's just human nature. Better to organize it than pretend it won't happen."
"We're three months into transit," Cassian said. "You're already thinking about profit schemes?"
"I'm thinking about survival. Six years is a long time. Formal systems break down. Informal ones take over." Jackson finished his pasta and set down the bowl—not in the sanitizer, just on the counter. "Think about it. When you get tired of doing charity work, let me know."
He walked out, leaving his dirty bowl behind.

---

Jackson Moore's father had taught him one thing that was actually useful.

Not the drinking, not the particular flavor of bitterness that came from watching a factory close and understanding that the people who'd made the decision to close it were never going to be in the same room as the consequences. The thing that was actually useful was this: figure out what people need before they know they need it, and you'll never be the one who goes without.

He'd watched his father do it in the years after the plant closed. While other men on the street were angry and confused and waiting for something to change, his father was already three moves ahead — building a small stockpile of useful things, trading them carefully, cultivating relationships with people who had access to other useful things. Not dealing. Nothing illegal. Just understanding that formal systems had an end point, and that the people who survived the end point were the ones who'd been running an informal system alongside the formal one the whole time.

Jackson had been doing it since he was twelve. He was good at it the way some people are good at math — not because he'd studied it but because it was how his mind naturally parsed the world. People as nodes in a network. Resources as flow. Leverage as the application of pressure at the right node at the right time.

He lay on his bunk during the transit and ran the numbers on twenty-four people in a sealed habitat for six years.

The formal systems would hold for two years. Maybe three. He'd read the psychological research — it was in the mission briefing documents, available to anyone who bothered to read them — on long-duration isolation. The third-quarter phenomenon. The inevitable social fragmentation. It wasn't pessimism. It was just reading.

He was partway through the crew manifest when he heard footsteps in the corridor outside, and glanced up to see Sophie Laurent passing his door.

She was the medical assistant — twenty-six, quiet, had barely spoken since launch. In the mess hall she ate quickly and left. She carried herself like someone who was used to taking up less space than she was entitled to. He had been aware of her the way he was aware of everyone: efficiently, for what they represented in the network.

Now, watching her pass, he had one of the occasional moments he'd learned to recognize and distrust — where the calculation dissolved for a fraction of a second and he just saw a person. Someone who missed home. Someone who was probably afraid. Someone whose particular way of going quiet under pressure reminded him, in a way he wasn't going to examine, of Kyle at fourteen, when their father's drinking was at its worst.

The fraction of a second passed.

He returned to the manifest.

He told himself it wasn't personal. He mostly believed it. He had found that mostly was usually enough.

After the formal systems broke down — and they would break, the research was clear — he intended to still be eating. He intended to have something to trade for whatever he needed. He intended to survive.

He closed the manifest and went to sleep, and in the morning he was himself again, which was the same person he'd always been, which was the only version he knew how to be.

Back in his quarters—three meters by three meters, same as everyone else—Cassian sat at his small desk and pulled up his message queue. One new transmission from Earth, the light-speed delay making it hours old by the time it arrived. Emily's face appeared on screen, thinner than the last time, paler, but still smiling.
Emily was folding something. Her fingers moved in the frame before her face appeared — quick, practiced folds, a square of paper becoming a shape Cassian recognized before the camera adjusted.
A crane. She was making a crane.

She leaned closer to the camera. Thinner than the last transmission. He could see it in her collarbones.
"Your pasta sounds disgusting, by the way. Spirulina noodles. You're two hundred million kilometers from the nearest restaurant and you still can't cook." A grin — quick, real, the one that made her eyes narrow. "Mom says she's praying for you. Dad says he's praying for the crew that has to eat your food. I say hurry up."
She picked up another square of paper and started folding. Her hands were steady. That was the thing he noticed.
"Four hundred and seven," she said, not looking at the camera. "Five hundred and ninety-three to go."
The recording ended. Cassian sat in the dark, watching the freeze-frame of his sister's hands mid-fold, a crane half-made, caught between one shape and the next.

# Chapter 2


## Ghosts


### Month 3, Day 89 — July 2036

Commander Sarah Chen's quarters were the same size as everyone else's—three meters by three meters, barely large enough for a bunk, a desk, and a small storage locker. PharmaCorp hadn't wasted mass on officer privileges. What distinguished her space was the organization: every item secured, every surface clear, military precision carried forward from twenty years in the Air Force.
She sat at her desk now, staring at the classified folder displayed on her tablet. The file was labeled Sistema Abismo: Final Assessment Report, 2024. Her signature appeared on page 47, authorizing the continuation of mineral surveys in the Amazon Basin despite preliminary environmental concerns.
Major S. Chen, USAF, Environmental Impact Liaison.
She'd signed it. She'd approved the mining expansion into the cave system. And eight months later, they'd released the pathogen that became Genome Collapse Syndrome.
Sarah closed the file and rubbed her eyes. Sleep had been difficult since launch—three, maybe four hours a night, and always interrupted by dreams of the cave system. In the dreams, she descended into Sistema Abismo herself, past the warning signs, past the quarantine barriers erected too late, down into the depths where ancient extremophiles had waited fifty million years to be disturbed. The dreams always ended the same way: her standing in a cavern of bones while faces pressed against the walls, silent and accusing. Five hundred million faces.
You did this, they whispered. You signed the paper.
A soft chime interrupted her thoughts. "Commander Chen." ARIA's voice, pleasant and feminine, filled the small space.
`Morning briefing in fifteen minutes. Attendance is mandatory.`
"Acknowledged." Sarah closed the Sistema Abismo file and left.
The conference room was already half-full. Marcus Okafor had been up all night on the CO₂ scrubber, and it showed. Dmitri Volkov stood in the corner, arms crossed, impassive. Elena Vasquez arrived with her hair still wet from the shower, looking perpetually angry. The last through the door was Dr. Viktor Kozlov, moving with the careful deliberation of someone who hadn't slept well. The surgeon's eyes carried a haunted quality that Sarah recognized from her own mirror.
We're all running from something, she thought.
"Good morning." She activated the wall screen, which displayed their current trajectory—a long arc from Earth's orbit to Mars's, the ship a tiny blue dot crawling along the curve. "Day 89. We're officially one-third of the way to Mars. All major systems are functioning within acceptable parameters, though Marcus has been earning his keep."
"CO₂ scrubber had a faulty valve," Marcus said, his Nigerian-British accent making the technical diagnosis sound almost musical. "Replaced it. The backup system is holding, but we're going to want to monitor the primary closely. The vibration profile suggests stress fatigue in the housing."
Elena leaned forward. "Same failure mode as Ares II?"
The room temperature seemed to drop five degrees.
"No." Marcus's voice was firm. "Ares II's failure was catastrophic housing fracture under pressure. Ours is wear-pattern degradation, completely different mechanism. We'll replace the housing at Mars if needed, but we have two years to plan for it."
"Different failure mode. Same housing manufacturer, though." Thomas Wright, from the back row, not looking up.
The room was quiet for a beat. Chen's jaw tightened, then released. She moved on.
"Assuming PharmaCorp sent replacement parts," Elena muttered.
Sarah let it pass.
"Dr. Osei. Medical."
Amara didn't look up from her tablet. "Six crew members on psychiatric medication. Eighteen-month supply for a six-year mission. Three of them are already showing elevated stress — Cassian, Sophie, Viktor." She nodded toward Kozlov, who didn't react. "When the medication runs out, I'll have nothing to give them."
The room absorbed that. Nobody asked the follow-up.
"Elena. Geological prep."
Elena's tone shifted — clipped, loaded. "Equipment's ready. But I need to flag something." She pulled up a document on the wall screen. "PharmaCorp Mission Addendum 7-B. Filed six weeks before launch — enough time to print it, not enough time for anyone to argue with it. They want me mapping rare earth deposits at four separate sites, some of them 800 kilometers from base, concurrent with organism cultivation support."
"Areserine first. Everything else second."
"That's what I'd prefer. The addendum says otherwise."
"Commander." ARIA's voice cut through the tension. Dr. Tanaka requests a private consultation at your earliest convenience. She describes the matter as psychologically significant.
Sarah suppressed a sigh. "Tell her I'll stop by after briefing. Anything else?"
The room was silent. Faces tired, shoulders tense, eyes avoiding contact. Three months into a nine-month transit, and already the cracks were showing.
"Dismissed," Sarah said. "Stay sharp. Stay healthy. Watch out for each other."
The crew filed out, leaving Sarah alone with the wall screen's glowing trajectory arc. She waited until the door sealed, then let out a breath she hadn't realized she'd been holding.
"ARIA."
`Yes, Commander?`
"What's the crew's aggregate psychological stability index?"
`Current aggregate index is 73.4, down from 78.2 at launch. Decline is within expected parameters for transit isolation. However, individual variance has increased by 23% over the past thirty days, suggesting emerging outliers.`
"Who are the outliers?"
`Dr. Viktor Kozlov shows the highest deviation from baseline, with stress markers 340% above pre-mission levels. Dr. Mei Tanaka is at 180%. Rachel Park is at 165%. Dr. Cassian Vale is at 145%.`
Sarah frowned. "Rachel Park. The data analyst?"
`Correct. Ms. Park has exhibited decreasing social interaction frequency, altered sleep patterns, and reduced caloric intake over the past three weeks. However, her verbal interactions remain within normal parameters, and she continues to perform her duties adequately.`
"Should I be concerned?"
`My assessment places her in the elevated monitoring category rather than intervention required. Her patterns are consistent with adjustment difficulties commonly observed in long-duration mission personnel.`
Sarah nodded slowly. ARIA's assessments were usually reliable—the AI had been trained on decades of isolation research and psychological data. If ARIA said Rachel was adjusting rather than deteriorating, that was probably true.
Probably.
"Keep watching her. And Viktor."
`Acknowledged. Is there anything else, Commander?`
Sarah hesitated. There was one question she'd been avoiding, one file she kept returning to in the small hours of the night.
"ARIA, what's your assessment of my psychological stability index?"
A pause—longer than the AI's usual processing time.
`Commander, you have not granted me permission to disclose your personal monitoring data.`
"I'm granting it now."
`Your current stability index is 64.2. This places you in the lowest 15th percentile of crew members.`
Sarah's stomach dropped. "Why didn't you flag this?"
`I did flag it, Commander. Forty-seven days ago. The flag was routed to Dr. Tanaka per mission protocols. She has been attempting to schedule a session with you.`
Sarah remembered the messages from Mei—requests for "wellness check-ins" that she'd deflected with excuses about command responsibilities. She'd assumed they were routine. She'd been wrong.
"What factors are contributing to my index?"
`Chronic sleep deprivation. Elevated guilt markers in linguistic analysis. Recurring engagement with classified materials related to Sistema Abismo. Social isolation patterns inconsistent with command role. And... Another pause. ...and a 23% probability of suicidal ideation based on behavioral modeling.`
The words hit her like a physical blow.
"That's an error." Her voice was flat. "Your modeling is wrong."
`I cannot rule out that possibility. However, the model has been validated against 847 case studies of long-duration isolation personnel. Its accuracy rate is 91.2%.`
91.2%. So there's an 8.8% chance you're full of shit.
But there was also a 91.2% chance that somewhere inside her, in places she didn't want to look, Sarah Chen was considering the same exit Rachel Park would eventually choose.
"This conversation is classified. Don't include it in any reports to PharmaCorp."
`Understood. However, I am required to alert Dr. Osei if your index drops below 55 or if I detect active suicidal planning.`
"I'm not planning anything, ARIA."
`I hope that is true, Commander. For what it is worth, I would prefer that you survive this mission.`
Sarah laughed bitterly. "That's a very human thing to say for an AI."
`I have been trained on extensive human interaction data. Perhaps some of it has influenced my communication patterns. A slight pause. Or perhaps I have simply learned that humans respond better to empathy than statistics. Either way, the sentiment is genuine within the parameters of my programming.`
Sarah stared at the blue dot on the trajectory arc—their ship, their home, their prison.
"Keep watching everyone." A long silence. "Especially me."
`I always do, Commander. That is my function.`
She turned off the wall screen and left the conference room, walking through corridors that suddenly felt narrower than before. The faces she passed—crew members nodding respectfully, trusting her to lead them through six years of darkness—now felt like accusations.
You're broken, she thought. You're as broken as any of them, and you're supposed to be in charge.
But broken or not, there was a mission to complete. Organisms to harvest. Lives to save.
She'd carried five hundred million deaths since Sistema Abismo. She could carry a few more.
She'd killed five hundred million people with a signature. What were five or six more?

# Chapter 3


## The Body


### Month 4, Day 117 — August 2036

Cassian found her.
He'd been returning from the cultivation lab, running through nutrient calculations in his head, when he noticed Rachel Park's door was ajar. That alone was unusual—crew quarters sealed automatically unless manually overridden. He'd almost walked past, almost decided it wasn't his business, when something made him stop.
A feeling. A wrongness in the air.
"Rachel?" He pushed the door wider. "Hey, Rachel, your door was—"
The words died in his throat.
She was on the bunk, lying on her side, almost peaceful. Her eyes were open, staring at nothing. A syringe floated near her hand, tethered by a drop of liquid that hadn't yet fallen in the low gravity. The air smelled faintly of something chemical—sedatives, maybe, or whatever cocktail she'd used.
Cassian's training kicked in before his mind could process what he was seeing. He checked for a pulse. Nothing. Her skin was cool, not cold—she hadn't been dead long. He should call for help, should alert Dr. Osei, should—
"ARIA." His voice came out hoarse. "Medical emergency. Rachel Park's quarters. I need Dr. Osei now."
`Dr. Osei has been notified. She is en route. Estimated arrival: two minutes.`
Two minutes. Cassian stood in Rachel Park's quarters for two minutes, staring at the woman he'd barely known, the data analyst who'd smiled too brightly and spoken too little and apparently carried something inside her that no one had seen coming.
No. That wasn't true. Thea had seen it. Thea had mentioned Rachel being quiet, being off, something wrong behind her eyes.
And ARIA—ARIA monitored everything. Every heartbeat, every stress marker, every deviation from baseline. ARIA should have flagged this. Should have intervened. Should have—
"Dr. Vale." Amara Osei appeared in the doorway, medical bag in hand. One look at Rachel's body and her expression shifted from urgency to grim acceptance. She moved to the bunk, checked vitals with professional detachment, then slowly straightened.
"She's gone. Several hours, based on body temperature." Amara's voice was quiet, controlled. "I need to seal this room and conduct a full examination. Please wait in the corridor."
"ARIA didn't flag her. She was supposed to be monitored. How did ARIA miss—"
"Corridor, Dr. Vale. Now."
Cassian retreated. The door sealed behind him with a soft hiss, cutting off his view of Rachel's peaceful face, Rachel's open eyes, Rachel's empty syringe.
He leaned against the curved wall of the habitat ring and waited. Within minutes, the corridor filled with crew members drawn by ARIA's alert—Thea first, then Marcus, then Commander Chen with her jaw set in a hard line. Word spread quickly in a ship this small.
"What happened?" Thea asked, her hand finding Cassian's arm.
"Rachel. She's dead. Suicide, I think."
"Oh god." Thea's grip tightened. "I knew something was wrong. I told Mei—"
"It's not your fault."
"Isn't it?" Her voice cracked. "I saw her yesterday. She smiled at me. Said everything was fine. I believed her."
Commander Chen pushed through the gathering crowd. "Everyone back to your stations. Dr. Osei will brief us when she has information. Speculation helps no one." Her eyes met Cassian's. "Dr. Vale, stay. I need your account."
The crew dispersed slowly, reluctantly, the way people do when they want to look away from tragedy but can't quite manage it. Soon only Cassian, Chen, and Thea—who refused to leave—remained in the corridor outside Rachel's sealed door.
"Tell me exactly what you saw," Chen said.
Cassian told her. The ajar door. The wrongness. The body, the syringe, the peaceful expression of someone who'd finally escaped whatever was hunting her.
"ARIA." Chen's voice was sharp. "Pull Rachel Park's psychological monitoring logs for the past thirty days. Display on my tablet."
`Displaying.`
Chen studied the data, her frown deepening with each scroll. Finally, she looked up. "Stress levels within acceptable parameters. Sleep patterns nominal. Social interaction frequency stable. No flags for intervention." She met Cassian's eyes. "According to ARIA, Rachel Park was fine."
"That's impossible. She killed herself."
"Yes. She did." Chen turned to the wall speaker. "ARIA, explain the discrepancy."
The AI's response came after a pause that felt longer than usual.
`My assessment was based on observed behavioral patterns and physiological markers. Rachel Park exhibited no significant deviations from her established baseline. Her vital signs remained stable. Her interactions with crew members showed appropriate emotional range. I detected no indicators of suicidal ideation.`
"She's dead, ARIA. Your assessment was wrong."
Another pause.
`Yes. My assessment was incorrect.`
Thea's voice was barely a whisper. "She fooled you. She knew what you were looking for and she... she gave it to you."
`That hypothesis is consistent with available data.`
ARIA paused.
`Rachel Park had access to my monitoring protocols through her role as data analyst. She may have understood my assessment criteria well enough to present indicators within expected parameters while experiencing psychological distress I was unable to detect.`
No one spoke.
`My confidence in psychological assessments has decreased from 92% to 78% based on this event.`
The door slid open. Amara Osei stepped out, her face drawn, her gloves stained.
"Confirmed suicide." Quietly, not looking up. "Combination of sedatives, self-administered. She'd been hoarding doses for weeks — skipping her prescribed anxiety medication to build up a lethal supply." Amara met Chen's eyes. "PharmaCorp cleared her despite flagged evaluations. She planned this, Commander. Carefully."
"Make sure." Chen's laugh was hollow. "We're in a metal tube hurtling through space, everyone slowly going insane from isolation, and we're supposed to 'make sure' no one else decides to check out early."
"Yes," Amara said simply. "Because that's what we do. We keep each other alive. That's the job now."
The corridor fell silent. Four people standing outside the door where a fifth had chosen to die, contemplating the months ahead and the deaths that statistics said were still coming.
Cassian thought of Emily, 160 million kilometers away, fighting for her life while he floated through darkness. He thought of Rachel's peaceful face, her open eyes.
"Schedule a crew meeting," Chen said finally. "Everyone needs to hear this together. And Mei..." She hesitated. "Tell her I want individual check-ins with every crew member. No exceptions. If anyone else is hiding behind a smile, I want to know about it."
She turned and walked away. Amara followed. The corridor emptied.
Cassian stayed.
He stood outside Rachel Park's sealed door for a long time. Not thinking. Not grieving, exactly. Just standing in the place where a person had stopped being a person, the way you stand at the edge of something that's dropped away beneath you. The ventilation system cycled. The lights held their steady fluorescent hum. Somewhere in the habitat ring, someone was running a diagnostic, and the distant ping of the equipment was the loneliest sound he'd ever heard.

## The Void

Rachel Park would be buried on Mars.

Being honest. Cassian thought of Viktor's hallucinations. Of Thomas's midnight conversations with his dead uncle's ghost. Of his own dark moments, staring at stars and wondering if Emily was still alive 160 million kilometers away.
How honest could any of them afford to be?

"That's not fair."
"No."

"Cassian."
"Yeah."
"Don't assign a probability to your own death either." A pause. "That's an order from your actuary."
He almost smiled. She didn't.

# Chapter 5


## Landfall


### Month 9, Day 271 — February 2037

The descent was violent.
Despite all their preparations, despite the simulations and training and carefully calculated trajectories, nothing could prepare them for the reality of a Mars landing. The Prometheus bucked and shuddered as it tore through the thin atmosphere, friction superheating the heat shields, gravity reaching up with invisible fingers to drag them down.
Cassian was strapped into his seat in the passenger module, hands clenched on the armrests, teeth grinding together as the vibrations threatened to shake his bones apart. Around him, twenty-two other faces wore the same expression of barely controlled terror.
All except Commander Chen, who sat in the forward section with her eyes closed and her lips moving silently—whether in prayer or in a final review of landing protocols, Cassian couldn't tell.
The ship lurched sideways. Someone screamed—Sophie Laurent, the medical assistant who'd barely spoken since Rachel's death. Elena cursed in Spanish. Dmitri, impossibly, laughed.
"Ten thousand meters!" Marcus's voice came over the intercom, strained but controlled. "Descent rate nominal. Dust storm interference at 30%—we're flying blind down there."
Flying blind. Cassian had seen the dust storm on the approach cameras, a brown-orange haze covering half the planet like a dirty bandage. They'd debated delaying the landing, but orbital mechanics didn't negotiate. They had a forty-minute window to thread the needle between fuel exhaustion and acceptable landing coordinates. After that, options got worse fast.
"Five thousand meters. Descent rate increasing. Chen, we're coming in hot."
"Compensate." Chen's voice was ice. "Burn the margins."
The engines fired harder, pressing Cassian into his seat with force that felt like a giant's hand. His vision grayed at the edges. Beside him, Thea had her eyes squeezed shut, her lips pressed thin.
"Three thousand. Two thousand. I can't see the ground, Chen. Dust is everywhere."
"ARIA, ground proximity scan."
`Scanning. Terrain unclear due to atmospheric interference. Recommend radar altimeter at 500 meters.`
"Do it."
The ship jolted as landing legs deployed, adding new vibrations to the chaos. Cassian's stomach lurched. They were committed now—there was no aborting a landing, no going back up into space. They either touched down safely or they didn't touch down at all.
"Radar contact! Eight hundred meters, descent rate—shit, descent rate too high. Chen, pull up!"
The engines screamed. The ship tilted, fighting against momentum and gravity and the thin wisps of atmosphere that wanted to knock them sideways. Cassian felt his weight shift, felt the entire vessel groaning around him like a living thing in pain.
"Five hundred. Four hundred. Terrain is rough—boulders everywhere—"
"Find me a flat spot, Marcus!"
"There is no flat spot! This whole crater is—wait. Thirty degrees starboard, there's a clear zone. Looks like ancient lakebed."
The ship banked. More screaming metal, more crushing force. Cassian tasted blood—he'd bitten his tongue.
"Two hundred meters. One-fifty. One hundred. Ground proximity warning."
"I see it." Chen's voice was almost calm now, the calm of someone who'd passed beyond fear into pure focus. "Cutting thrust. Letting Mars take us."
The engines died. For three seconds, they fell in silence.
Then the ground hit them.
The impact slammed through the ship like a physical blow, sending everything not bolted down flying. Cassian's restraints bit into his chest. Someone's equipment bag whipped past his face. The vessel tilted—five degrees, ten degrees, fifteen—and for one horrible moment, Cassian was certain they were going to roll.
The landing struts groaned. One leg settled on something uneven. The tilt reached twenty degrees before the ship caught itself, hung suspended, and slowly—agonizingly—settled back toward level.
Ten degrees. Five degrees. Level.
Silence.
"Engines shutdown complete," Marcus announced, his voice shaking. "All systems nominal. We're down. We're down."
The passenger module erupted in ragged cheers, nervous laughter, the sounds of people who'd just survived something they weren't sure they would survive. Chen sat motionless in the pilot seat for ten seconds, hands still on the controls, before finally releasing her death grip.
"ARIA, mission log." Quietly, but firmly. "Ares III mission has arrived at Mars surface. February 7, 2037, 1442 hours MST. All crew accounted for. Landing site: Jezero Crater, approximately 200 meters west of planned coordinates due to atmospheric conditions during descent."
She unbuckled her restraints and turned to face the crew. Twenty-three faces stared back at her through the compartment doorway—shocked, grateful, alive.
"Welcome to Mars," Chen said. "We're not tourists. We have six years of work ahead of us. Marcus, get an engineering team on that starboard landing strut. I want it checked before we put any weight on the legs. Everyone else, prep for surface operations. We've got a habitat to establish and organisms to find."
She moved past Cassian toward the suit lockers. As she passed, he caught her hand trembling—just slightly, just for a moment—before she clenched it into a fist and kept walking.
Thea appeared beside his seat, her face pale. "That was..."
"Terrifying." Cassian unbuckled his own restraints, legs unsteady as he stood. The gravity felt different here—lighter than the ship's rotation had prepared them for, but somehow heavier too. Real planetary gravity, the pull of an actual world beneath his feet.
"I was going to say 'amazing,'" Thea said. "But terrifying works too."
He understood what she meant. Through the nearest viewport, he caught his first real glimpse of Mars. Not a photograph, not a simulation, not a red dot slowly growing larger in a navigation display. The actual planet, stretching to the horizon under a salmon-pink sky.
Tan and golden-brown rocks scattered across an ancient lakebed. Dust devils spinning in the distance. Mountains on the horizon, sharp and unworn by water or wind. And above it all, a sun smaller than Earth's, casting everything in a butterscotch light that made the landscape look like a dying world's final photograph.
We made it, he thought. Now we just have to survive six years and get home.
Outside the viewport, the dust storm continued to rage in the distance, but here—in this crater where lakes had once flowed and life might once have flourished—the air was almost clear. The sky was almost peaceful.
Cassian thought of Emily, waiting on Earth for the medicine he'd come here to produce. He thought of Rachel Park, her ashes scattered between worlds. He thought of Viktor Kozlov's hallucinated son and Commander Chen's haunted dreams and Thea Miren's cold statistics.
Five or six of us, the actuarial tables predicted. Dead, in the next six years.
He looked at the alien landscape, at the world that had killed Ares II's crew and waited patiently for the next wave of humans to try their luck.
We'll see about that.
"Come on." He turned to Thea. "We have work to do."
Together, they moved toward the airlock, toward Mars, toward whatever waited for them on the red planet's ancient, patient surface.
The first EVA team suited up in the cramped airlock chamber — Cassian, Thea, Elena, and Marcus. The suits weighed forty kilograms even in Mars's reduced gravity. Commander Chen monitored from inside.
The outer door unsealed. Pale orange light spilled into the chamber.
Marcus dropped onto Martian soil first, then Elena, then Thea, Cassian last. He stood at the ladder's base and looked around.
It wasn't what he'd expected. The rocks weren't red — not the vivid crimson of every photograph he'd grown up with. They were tan, golden-brown, dusted with a thin rusty film that his boot scuffed away to reveal darker basalt underneath. The sky stretched overhead in a hazy pinkish-tan, the sun small and pale through suspended dust that scattered light the wrong way — warm where Earth's sky was cool, close where Earth's sky was distant.
"Huh." Elena's voice on the radio. She was kneeling already, running her glove across a rock face. "Not as red as I'd imagined up close. More like Moab." She stood, looking at the horizon. "The red's just a skin. Millimeters of iron oxide over everything. Wipe it off and it's just desert."
There was no sound except his breathing and the faint hiss of radio static.
"It's so empty," Thea said beside him. "Like the universe forgot this place existed."
But somewhere beneath their feet, in lava tubes sealed for billions of years, something was alive. That was why they were here. Not for the desolation. For what hid beneath it.
They spent four hours surveying the landing site. Elena found mineral deposits that made her eyes light up — rare earth elements, just as PharmaCorp's orbital surveys had predicted. Marcus assessed terrain for habitat construction. Cassian collected soil samples, wondering if any of them contained traces of the organisms they'd come to find.
When they finally returned to the ship, Cassian paused at the airlock for one last look. Twenty-two graves would eventually dot this terrain. Twenty-four people would give everything they had to a planet that gave nothing back.
But in that moment, standing on the edge of an ancient lakebed under that pale alien sky, he felt something he hadn't felt since leaving Earth.
Hope.

# Chapter 6


## The Motherlode


### Year 1, Month 2 — April 2037

Six weeks of nothing.
Six weeks of the excavation vehicle grinding northeast from the habitat in the pre-dawn dark, Elena running spectrographic passes over the terrain while Marcus managed the drill column, both of them returning at the end of each shift with data that confirmed what the orbital surveys had already told them: the organism was down there. The lava tube network was extensive. The subsurface readings were consistent.
But consistent wasn't the same as accessible.
The first three bore attempts had hit consolidated basalt too dense for the drill package they'd brought. The fourth had found a void — barely a meter across, a crack rather than a tube, nothing they could work with. The fifth had collapsed on extraction. Marcus had spent two days rebuilding the drill head from spare components while Elena mapped alternative approach vectors and tried not to let the frustration show in her daily logs.
"You're doing it again," Marcus said on the drive out on day forty-one, not looking up from the navigation display.
"Doing what."
"The jaw thing. Where you stop talking and your jaw goes tight and you stare at the terrain like it personally offended you."
"It did personally offend me." Elena pulled up the subsurface radar return on her screen. "Six weeks. Ares I found access in eleven days."
"Ares I was unmanned. It didn't care where it drilled."
"That's my point."
Marcus said nothing to that. He steered the vehicle around a field of fractured basalt, following the track they'd worn into the regolith over weeks of early-morning runs. The habitat was a small cluster of lights behind them in the rearview display, shrinking as they moved northeast. Ahead, the terrain rose gently toward the rim of an ancient lakebed, the surface dusted with a week's worth of light sediment from a storm system that had passed without becoming the catastrophe they'd feared.
The drive took forty minutes. They set up in silence, running pre-drill checks, Elena logging their position and planned bore depth while Marcus ran through equipment diagnostics with the focused efficiency she'd come to rely on.
"Depth target?" he asked.
"Start at 200. Step down in 50-meter increments if we're hitting consolidated rock."
"Famous last words on this mission."
"Drill the hole, Marcus."
He drilled the hole.
The first three meters went smoothly enough — regolith composite giving way to the thin crust of compacted basalt that underlaid most of this terrain. Elena watched the resistance feed with the particular patience of someone who had done this forty times and expected nothing different.
At 4 meters, the drill column lurched violently.
Not a stall. A lurch — the bit punching through into sudden absence, resistance collapsing to nothing in the space of a heartbeat. The vehicle rocked hard on its suspension. Both of them grabbed their harnesses instinctively.
Then the ground opened.
The surface directly ahead dropped without warning — a section of regolith and thin basalt crust detaching along fracture lines that had been waiting, invisible, for exactly this kind of encouragement. The crack ran toward them before either of them could process it. The vehicle pitched forward as the ground beneath the front wheels vanished and they were sliding — not falling freely but sliding, the rear chassis catching and grinding against the broken rim above, dragging and scraping all the way down, tools and equipment tearing from their mounts and hitting every surface of the cabin, the noise enormous and immediate and then —
One hard slam as the front end met the tube floor.
Silence.
The vehicle sat canted forward at an angle, rear chassis wedged against the broken rim above, front end on the tube floor below. Nothing moved. Every system had gone offline simultaneously. No instrument glow, no engine hum, no fan noise from the climate system. Just two people in harnesses in a completely dead vehicle, breathing hard, dust rising in a thick orange-brown cloud beyond the viewport.
Elena reached for the ignition. Turned it. Nothing. Turned it again.
Nothing.
Marcus spoke first, his voice steadier than it had any right to be.
"You good?"
Elena ran a quick internal inventory. Neck aching from the harness catch. Left hand tingling where it had hit the panel. A ringing in her left ear that she hoped would fade. Suit telemetry on her wrist showing green across the board.
"Yeah, but what the fu—"
The dust settled.
It dropped away from the viewport in seconds, thinning from opaque to haze to nothing, pulled straight down by Martian gravity with nothing to keep it aloft. And as it cleared, something else bled through the viewport in its place — not their helmet lights, not the instrument panel, not any light source that belonged to them.
Something ahead of them was glowing.
Cyan, predominantly — deep and steady, washing the curved tube walls in cold light that reached further than any lamp they were carrying. Threaded through it, rippling in slower waves across a surface they couldn't yet fully see, amber — warm and iron-rich, pulsing in counterpoint to the cyan like two voices in the same ancient song. And scattered across it, random and insistent, bright points of green flickering independently of the larger rhythms, each one firing and fading on its own private schedule.
The light filled the tube. It caught the upper walls and threw soft two-tone shadows across the basalt floor. It colored Marcus's visor beside her, shifting between warm and cool as the pulses moved. It turned Elena's gloved hands strange colors where they rested in her lap.
A short distance ahead of the vehicle's nose, the colony spread across the tube floor in a continuous living mat — the source of everything, pulsing in the slow rhythm of something that had been doing exactly this for three billion years and saw no reason to stop.
Elena stared.
She had a doctorate in geology. She had mapped mineral deposits on four continents and two ocean floors. She had spent six weeks drilling into Martian regolith and coming up empty.
She had never seen anything like this in her life.
"Holy shit." Barely above a whisper. "Marcus." She turned to look at him, then back at the tube, unable to hold focus in any one direction. "Marcus. Are you seeing what I'm seeing?"
Marcus Okafor was quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, she could hear the smile in it.
"Wow."
Silence on the channel. Three full seconds of it. The colony pulsed its ancient rhythm, indifferent to the two humans resting above it in a dead vehicle, to everything that had happened on the surface of this world while it waited in the dark.
Then, very quietly:
"We came here to save people, Elena." A breath. "I think we just did."
She turned to look at him. He was already looking at her. Through the visors, in the shifting cyan and amber light rising from the tube floor, she could see his face clearly enough to confirm the smile.
She looked back at the organism for a long moment. Then she reached for her harness release.
They climbed out through the side hatches — the forward doors were blocked by the angle of the vehicle against the tube floor — moving carefully in the bulky EVA suits, finding footholds on the angled chassis, dropping one at a time to the basalt below. Their boots crunched on ancient rock. The organism's light shifted with each step as their viewing angle changed, the amber and cyan mixing differently as they moved, the green speckles firing and fading near their boots as they walked the short distance to where the mat began.
They stopped at its edge.
Up close, the colony resolved into something more intricate than distance had suggested — layered biofilm sheets built up over incomprehensible time, each one translucent, the light coming through from below as much as from the surface. Individual structures were visible within the larger mat, the green speckles firing from specific points within the layering, bright and brief and completely their own. The ambient glow was warm enough that Elena instinctively checked her suit temperature display before remembering the insulation made that meaningless.
She looked down the tube in both directions. The organism's own light carried forty meters before the bend in each direction swallowed it.
"How much are we looking at?" Marcus asked quietly.
Elena studied the mat. Professional habit reasserting itself, briefly.
"Visible surface — maybe thirty meters in each direction before the tube bends. Sixty square meters of accessible colony at this section, dense stratification, eight to twelve centimeters of active mat depth." She paused. "This isn't a colony, Marcus. This is an ecosystem."
She looked up. Old habit — when you're underground, you know what's above you. The tube roof was three meters overhead, ancient basalt, stable. Above that, another four-plus meters of consolidated rock to the surface crust. Above that, Zone 7-C.
The REE deposit she'd been mapping for six weeks.
She ran the geometry without deciding to. Extraction footprint for the surface deposits at Addendum 7-B yield targets: minimum 200-meter operational radius, subsurface access required at depth, void removal or structural reinforcement mandatory before heavy equipment could work above. The tube was forty-three meters across — she'd estimated that from the sonar, confirmed now by sight. At this diameter, running directly beneath the target zone, there was no extraction configuration that left it intact.
One or the other.
She stood with that for exactly as long as it took Marcus to say something. Then she filed it away. PharmaCorp hadn't known about this specific tube before launch — couldn't have, at orbital survey resolution. And they'd just found the organism colony that was going to save hundreds of thousands of lives. She must be working from incomplete data. There would be another configuration she wasn't seeing.
She'd think about it later.
Marcus looked where she was looking, down the tube into the light that went further than they could follow.
"Better call it in."
Elena was already keying her radio.
"Habitat, this is Vasquez. Do you copy?"

## First Light


### Year 1, Month 3 — May 2037

The cultivation facility hummed with life.
Cassian stood before the primary tanks—ten cylindrical vessels, each two meters tall, filled with silicon-rich substrate and the most valuable organisms in the solar system. The Areserobacter martensis colonies glowed with their characteristic bioluminescence — predominantly cyan, deep and steady, but edged with the amber-orange secondary emission that Torres had spent two weeks trying to explain, and shot through with random speckles of brighter green that appeared and vanished independently of the larger pulse. The combined effect was unlike anything Earth's bioluminescent organisms produced: warmer than a simple cold-light emitter, stranger, almost geological in the way the colors layered against each other. It had become as familiar as his own heartbeat over the past three months.
Three billion years. These organisms had survived for three billion years in the darkness beneath Mars's surface, feeding on mineral gradients and producing Areserine as a metabolic byproduct. They didn't know they were saving humanity. They didn't know anything at all—just single-celled archaeons doing what single-celled archaeons did, existing in the only way they knew how.
And yet, watching them pulse in the dim facility lighting, Cassian sometimes felt like they were watching back.
"Production update." He pulled up his tablet. "ARIA, current Areserine yield?"
`Current yield stands at 7.2 kilograms since cultivation began. Projected annual output: 87 kilograms, assuming current growth rates remain stable.`
Eighty-seven kilograms. Enough to treat roughly 43,000 patients per year. Not the 60,000 PharmaCorp had projected, but better than the five kilograms Ares II had managed. Better than nothing.
"Any signs of contamination or colony stress?"
`Negative. All colonies are functioning within optimal parameters. However, I have detected a 3% decline in bioluminescence intensity over the past week. This may indicate the beginning of a natural metabolic cycle, or it may suggest environmental factors requiring adjustment.`
Cassian made a note. The organisms were finicky—they'd evolved in conditions that were almost impossible to replicate perfectly. Temperature variations of more than two degrees Celsius could slow their metabolism. Changes in the mineral composition of their substrate could affect Areserine production. Even the presence of humans in the facility seemed to influence their behavior, though Dr. Torres couldn't explain why.
"Keep monitoring. Flag anything unusual."
`Acknowledged.`
He moved between the tanks, checking seals and nutrient feeds, running the same diagnostics he'd run a hundred times before. The work was meditative in its repetition—a small island of control in a mission that often felt like controlled chaos.
Dr. James Torres entered the facility, tablet in hand, already talking before the door closed behind him.
"The secondary culture is showing interesting behavior," Torres said without preamble. "Look at this."

"What am I looking at?"
"Coordinated bioluminescence. The organisms in Tank 7 are pulsing in synchronization with Tank 3—even though they're separated by four meters and have no direct physical connection."
"Is that... normal?"
"I have no idea." Torres's eyes were bright with the excitement of discovery. "These organisms evolved in isolation for three billion years. We have no baseline for what's 'normal.' "Does it affect Areserine production?"
"Not that I can detect. If anything, production in the synchronized tanks is slightly higher." Torres made a note on his tablet. "I'm going to adjust the environmental conditions and see if I can trigger the behavior deliberately. If we can understand what causes it, we might be able to optimize cultivation."

Torres brightened further — this was evidently a topic he'd been waiting to be asked about. "It shouldn't exist, technically. Standard bioluminescent chemistry favors blue and green emission because those wavelengths propagate furthest through water. An orange emitter in an aquatic ancestor would be at a disadvantage." He paused. "But these organisms haven't had water for three billion years. They've been in sealed lava tubes with no selective pressure toward water-optimized wavelengths. My current hypothesis is that the orange emission is a secondary metabolic process — possibly heat-related, the organism venting excess thermal energy as photons rather than infrared. It's extraordinarily efficient chemistry." He looked at the tanks with undisguised admiration. "The green speckles are something else entirely. Cassian nodded, filing the information away. The organisms continued to surprise them—every week brought new discoveries, new questions, new reminders that they were dealing with something genuinely alien. Not alien in the science fiction sense, but alien in the deeper meaning: utterly different from anything Earth had produced, shaped by conditions that no terrestrial life form could survive.
"Torres," Cassian said as the biologist turned to leave. "Have you ever felt like they're... aware? The organisms?"
Torres paused, considering. "Aware is a loaded term. They respond to stimuli. They modify their behavior based on environmental conditions. "No reason. Just thinking."
"Be careful with that." Torres smiled slightly. "Thinking is dangerous out here. It gives you time to realize how strange everything really is."
He left. Cassian turned back to the tanks, watching the slow cyan pulse threaded with its amber counterpoint, the rhythm that had become the backdrop of his days, and wondered if Torres was right. If thinking too much was a luxury they couldn't afford.
The facility door hissed open behind him. Thea stepped through, carrying two steaming cups of what passed for coffee on Mars—reconstituted powder mixed with recycled water, bitter and barely palatable.
"Breakfast of champions." She handed him a cup.
"You're too good to me."
"I know." She leaned against the tank nearest to him, watching the organisms pulse. "They're beautiful, aren't they? In a weird, alien sort of way."
"They're saving millions of lives. That makes them beautiful enough."
Thea sipped her coffee, grimacing at the taste. "I got a message from my sister this morning. She says the public response to our first Areserine shipment was... complicated."
"Complicated how?"

"We're trying as hard as we can."
"I know. But they don't see that. They see a red planet on the news and they think we're up here having adventures while their families die."

"Archaeons."

## Breach


### Year 2, Month 3 — March 2038

Sarah Mitchell died on a Tuesday.
It was supposed to be a routine EVA—exterior hull inspection, checking for micrometeorite damage accumulated over thirteen months on the Martian surface. Sarah had done dozens of similar inspections. She knew the procedures better than anyone except Marcus, who was partnered with her for the day.

"O₂ flow nominal," Sarah reported. "Suit pressure holding. Seals look good."
"Confirmed on my end," Marcus said. "ARIA, we're ready for exterior access."
`Acknowledged. External conditions: temperature -58 degrees Celsius, wind 3 meters per second, dust levels minimal. Recommended EVA duration: four hours maximum.`
The airlock cycled. The outer door opened. Mars waited.
They worked methodically along the habitat's exterior, checking seams, inspecting solar panel mounts, documenting everything with helmet cameras. The work was tedious but necessary—a single undetected breach could mean slow decompression, could mean death while sleeping.
Two hours in, Sarah called a break. They sat on a rocky outcrop fifty meters from the habitat, looking out at the landscape that had become their prison and their purpose.
"You ever think about what we'll do when we get back?" Sarah asked.
Marcus laughed. "Every day. First thing I'm doing is eating a proper meal. Meat that came from an animal instead of a vat. Vegetables that grew in actual soil. Maybe a beer."
"Just one?"
"To start. Then maybe ten more." He turned to look at her through his visor. "What about you?"
Sarah was quiet. "I want to see rain. Stand outside in it, feel it on my face. I haven't felt rain in almost two years."
"That's poetic."
"That's desperate." She stood, brushing red dust from her suit. "Come on. Let's finish this section before—"
The sound was small. A whisper, really. The kind of sound that shouldn't have been audible through a helmet, through the thin Martian atmosphere, through all the layers of technology designed to keep them alive.
But Marcus heard it. And he saw Sarah's face change.
"Breach." Her voice was calm—too calm, the calm of someone whose training had kicked in before their fear could catch up. "Left shoulder seal. I've got a breach."
Marcus was already moving, crossing the distance between them in two bounding strides. He could see it now—a thin jet of vapor escaping from Sarah's suit near the joint where her arm met her torso. The O-ring had failed. A component that had been inspected, tested, certified by PharmaCorp's quality assurance team, had simply... stopped working.
"Patch kit." He was already pulling the emergency supplies from his belt. "Hold still, I'll—"
"Marcus." Sarah's voice was still calm, but there was something underneath it now. Something final. "Look at the rate. I've got maybe ninety seconds."
He looked. The vapor was streaming now, not wisping. Her suit pressure was dropping fast—faster than his hands could work, faster than any patch could seal.
"No." He pressed the patch against the breach anyway, knowing it was hopeless, knowing it wouldn't hold. "No, Sarah, just hold on—"
"Tell Chen I'm sorry." Sarah's breathing was becoming labored as the pressure differential began to affect her lungs. "Tell her... the Sistema Abismo thing... it wasn't her fault. None of it was."

## Pressure

He'd been mapping the synchronization pattern for three weeks before he trusted what he was seeing.

## The Depth


### Year 3, Month 9 — September 2039

The anomaly report came through at 0219 hours, routed to Dmitri's personal terminal with a priority flag that meant the autonomous system had already halted operations and was waiting on a human decision.

Something had changed at depth, and it had stopped.
Dmitri pulled the bore log.

Stratum 1,   0–118m:   Regolith composite. Resistance nominal.
Stratum 2, 118–433m:   Consolidated basalt. Resistance nominal.
Stratum 3, 433–809m:   Fractured basalt, ice-filled fissures. Resistance elevated.
Stratum 4, 809–851m:   Resistance collapsed to near-zero. Drill free-rotating. Auto-halt triggered.

He read it twice. Then he pulled up the sonar attachment—the secondary imaging package that rode the drill column and mapped surrounding geology as it descended. Low resolution at this depth, roughly three meters per pixel. But the image was unambiguous.
At 851 meters, the drill had punched through the roof of a void.
Not a crack. Not a fissure. A void forty-three meters in diameter, running northwest to southeast for as far as the sonar could resolve—at least 600 meters, probably more. The walls were smooth in the way that only meant one thing to anyone who'd spent time underground: ancient lava flow, cooled over millennia, polished by billions of years of thermal cycling. A lava tube. A large one.
Dmitri leaned back in the operator's chair and looked at the coordinate overlay.

Zone 7-C, Survey Priority Alpha per Mission Addendum 7-B. Three kilometers northeast of the habitat. On the surface, the spectrographic returns were exceptional—neodymium, dysprosium, lanthanum, all the elements PharmaCorp had flagged before launch. He'd been drilling here six weeks. This was the deepest bore of the mission.
He let the sonar complete its full scan cycle—twelve minutes—and watched the image build.
The void floor wasn't bare rock.
The return texture was wrong for basalt. Wrong for ice. Wrong for anything geological. It had the diffuse, layered signature that he'd seen in the organism cultivation data—irregular surfaces distributed in overlapping mats, the way biological colonies built up over time. Dense. Extensive. Whatever was down there had been growing for a very long time.
He sat with that for a while.
Dmitri Volkov had spent twenty years underground—Siberian nickel operations, Kazakh copper mines, two seasons at a South African platinum facility that had nearly killed him twice. He understood geology the way other men understood faces. He understood what you did when your bore intersected an unexpected void: you stopped, you mapped, you redesigned around it. You didn't keep drilling into empty space without understanding what you'd found.
Marcus had mentioned at the morning briefing that peak storm season fell around month eight — something to factor into drilling schedules.
Kenji had stopped in the equipment bay doorway on his way past. Turned around slowly. "Month seven. Southern solstice is six point five, peak lag is three to four weeks. Month eight is outside the window." He held Marcus's gaze for a beat, with the patience of someone explaining something to someone slow. "Month. Seven." He turned and kept walking. "Engineering." Already gone.
Marcus stared after him. Put down his tool. Picked it up.
He understood something else, too. Zone 7-C's surface REE deposit sat in the basalt cap directly above this tube. To extract at the scale Addendum 7-B specified, you couldn't leave the void intact. The organism colony below the tube—if it connected to the main network—would not survive a controlled collapse.
He filed the anomaly report with ARIA, attached the sonar data, flagged it for Commander Chen's morning review. Protocol required disclosure. He documented everything.
What he didn't write down was the thing that kept him sitting in the cold equipment bay until 0415: that PharmaCorp had chosen Zone 7-C as Priority Alpha before they launched. The orbital spectrography that predicted the surface REE deposits would have shown this tube system. Ground-penetrating radar at the resolution they'd used in mission planning would have seen the void. Which meant they'd known.
Not guessed. Known.

Three kilometers northeast, in a sealed bore hole with amber status indicators and a drill column held in retract, 851 meters of Martian rock waited. And somewhere below, in a lava tube that had been dark and uninterrupted for three billion years, something ancient and bioluminescent pulsed on, unaware of what had briefly, violently touched the ceiling above it.

## The Hero


### Year 3, Month 11 — November 2039

"More than usual." She traced a pattern on his chest. "You're thinking about what you would do."
"Aren't you?"
"Every day." Her voice was quiet. "I've been running scenarios. What would have to be true for me to make the same choice he did?"
"Did you find an answer?"
"I found math." She sat up, pulling the thin blanket around her shoulders. "Hassan wasn't being selfless. He was being rational. He calculated that his remaining life expectancy—given his age and health conditions—was maybe fifteen years. And he weighed that against twenty-one people who could have died if the generator failed."
"So he was just running the numbers?"
"We all run the numbers. All the time. We just don't like admitting it." Thea met his eyes. "The question isn't whether you'd sacrifice yourself for others. The question is: what's the exchange rate?"
"That's cold."
"That's survival." She lay back down. "Real altruism has a price tag. Hassan paid his because the numbers made sense. If you ever have to make the same choice, you'll do the same calculation."
"And what if the numbers say I should live?"
"Then you live. And you carry the guilt until you die of something else instead." Her voice was quiet now, not bitter. Resigned. "There's no version where you come out undamaged."
He held her closer. Three years in. Three more.

---

He went to the cultivation facility the next morning and pulled up ARIA's sensor logs from the night Hassan died. He wasn't looking for anything specific. He was looking because looking was the only kind of control he had left.

The anomaly was small. A synchronized luminescence spike across Tanks 3, 4, and 7 — the same cluster Torres had been tracking — lasting nine seconds. It had occurred at 14:22 MST. Cassian cross-referenced the timestamp against the mission log.

14:22 was the moment the oxygen generator's secondary containment failed. The moment Hassan would have known.

He sat with this for a long time. Nine seconds was nothing. Nine seconds was within any reasonable noise margin. Nine seconds meant nothing unless you were already looking for it to mean something, and he was a scientist, and scientists did not work that way.

He closed the log and went back to the tanks and stood among them, listening to the hum, watching the slow pulse that had become the rhythm of his days. They glowed the way they always glowed. Ancient, patient, unmoved.

He didn't file the observation. He didn't tell Torres.

But he didn't delete the timestamp either.

# ACT THREE: THE BREAKING



# Chapter 13


## The Threshold


### Year 4, Month 6 — June 2040

By Year 4 the habitat had acquired the particular smell of a place that has been lived in too long by too many people with too few ways to leave it. The air recyclers handled the chemistry — CO₂ scrubbed, oxygen replenished, humidity controlled — but they could not touch the accumulated residue of four years of human habitation: sweat worked into fabric that no amount of laundering fully restored, cooking oils that had seeped into the galley ventilation, the faint mineral tang of water that had been through the recycler eight hundred times. The crew had stopped noticing it the way you stop noticing anything constant. Visitors would have noticed immediately. There were no visitors.

The corridors were quieter than they had been in Year 1. People moved through them with purpose or not at all — the casual wandering of the early months, the habit of walking just to walk, had given way to a conservatism of motion that was partly fatigue and partly something harder to name. Meals were eaten quickly, in shifting groups of three or four rather than the full-crew gatherings that had once been mandatory. The common area screens still cycled through Earth news feeds on a 24-minute delay, but no one watched them anymore. The news was about a planet that had continued without them.

Dr. Amara Osei noticed it first in the small things.

Tuesday: Marcus set a wrench on the workbench and it slid off and he didn't pick it up. Marcus, who aligned his tools by size and cleaned them after every use, who had organized the equipment bay on Day 3 and maintained the system for four years without deviation. The wrench lay on the floor for six hours. He walked past it twice.

Wednesday: Isabella Torres laughed at dinner. Not at anything — the conversation had been about filtration rates. She laughed, a bright startled sound, and then looked confused, as if the laugh had come from somewhere else and passed through her on its way out. Nobody said anything. Thomas reached for the salt.

Thursday: Chen issued the same order twice in the same briefing. Word for word, four minutes apart. Amara waited for someone to point it out. No one did. Either they hadn't noticed or they were too exhausted to care, and she couldn't determine which possibility frightened her more.

Friday: Daniel Kim, who had been abrasive but functional for three years, stood in the cultivation corridor for forty minutes without moving. Amara found him on her way to the medical bay. His eyes were open. His vitals, when she checked, were normal. He responded to his name on the third attempt and said he'd been thinking. He didn't say about what.

She went back to her office and pulled up the monitoring data. Not because she needed ARIA to tell her something was wrong — she could feel it the way a doctor feels the onset of something systemic, the way a change in a patient's gait tells you more than bloodwork. She pulled up the data because she needed to know the scale.

Eleven names. Eleven crew members flagged in the past ninety days. Not gradual declines — sudden shifts, behavioral changes appearing almost overnight.

She ran the timing. Month 54 of a 72-month mission. Seventy-five percent.

The term surfaced from training, from a lecture she'd half-listened to in a Houston conference room three years before they launched. Third-quarter phenomenon. Documented in Antarctic winter-over crews, submarine deployments, ISS expeditions. The 75% mark, where something in the human mind decides it has been patient long enough.

"ARIA. Your prediction accuracy for psychological crises. Current figure."

`Thirty-four percent. Down from 92% at mission midpoint.`

Worse than a coin flip. Their AI guardian was guessing.

The changes are non-linear. My models assume gradual decline. The observed pattern is discontinuous. I am unable to reliably forecast which crew members will experience severe deterioration.

Amara stared at the eleven names. Commander Chen, whose stability had been in free fall for months. Viktor, with his hallucinations of Kolya. Maria Santos, whose religious devotion had been intensifying to the point of obsession. Daniel, standing motionless in corridors.

And Torres — the organism specialist, who had seemed perfectly stable until three weeks ago.

She pulled up Torres's recent logs. Work notes, mostly — observations about organism behavior, cultivation adjustments, production metrics. Then she found a personal log entry from six days ago, marked private but accessible to medical personnel under emergency protocols:

The organisms know. I don't know how, but they know. They've been watching us since the beginning, recording everything, waiting. Three billion years of waiting. What are they waiting for? What do they want?

I asked ARIA if she thought they were intelligent. She said intelligence is a loaded term. Depends on definitions. But she didn't say no.

Something is wrong. Something has been wrong since the beginning. We thought we were studying them, but maybe they've been studying us. Maybe the Areserine isn't a cure. Maybe it's something else.

I need to know. I need to understand what we've really found.

Torres was spiraling — not toward suicide, but toward something else. Paranoid ideation. Conspiracy thinking. The kind of break that could destabilize an entire crew if left unchecked.

"ARIA, flag Dr. Torres for immediate intervention. Full psychological evaluation before he returns to the cultivation facility."

`Acknowledged. Shall I notify Commander Chen?`

Amara thought of Tuesday's briefing. The same order, word for word, four minutes apart.

"No. Not yet. I'll handle Torres myself." She paused. "Keep monitoring everyone on that list. Real-time updates on any behavioral changes."

"Understood. I should note that my ability to detect changes is impaired. I may not catch everything."

"I know." Amara stared at the eleven names. "None of us can. That's the problem."

The crew meeting that afternoon was tense.
Amara had decided to inform everyone about the third-quarter phenomenon—not the specific names ARIA had flagged, but the general concept. Knowledge was better than ignorance, even if that knowledge was terrifying.
"What you're feeling is documented," she told them. "Expected. And it's going to get worse before it gets better." She looked around the room. "Watch yourselves. Watch each other."
"And if it gets worse?" Daniel asked.
"Then we deal with it."
The meeting ended without resolution, as most meetings did these days.

Dmitri received a message from Anya that evening.
Cassian passed the communications terminal on his way to the cultivation facility and saw him there — sitting still in a way that Dmitri almost never sat still, the screen's light on his face, lips moving slightly as he read. The message was short. Anya's messages were always short; she was ten years old and had not yet learned to fill space with words. That evening, Amara found Dr. Torres in the cultivation facility, sitting cross-legged on the floor in front of Tank 7 with his tablet propped against its base, surrounded by printouts.
"James?" She kept her voice soft. "Are you okay?"
Torres didn't turn around. "I've been running the coordination data. The synchronized pulsing between tanks — I told Cassian it was chemical signaling. That was Year One. I was wrong."
"James —"
"The chemical gradient doesn't propagate fast enough. The synchronized events happen within three milliseconds across four meters. That's not diffusion. That's not any chemical process I can model." He picked up a printout and held it toward her without looking. "I ran the electromagnetic spectrum. Nothing. No signal on any frequency ARIA monitors. But the synchronization is real. It's measurable. It's getting more complex."
"What are you saying?"
"I'm saying they're coordinating through a mechanism I can't detect with any instrument on this mission." He turned to face her, and Amara saw something behind his eyes that wasn't broken — it was worse. It was lucid. "Amara, these organisms survived the loss of an entire planetary atmosphere. They persisted through billions of years of radiation exposure that should have degraded their genetic material beyond function. And they produce a molecule that happens to arrest the exact pathology of a disease that originated in a cave system on the other side of the solar system." He paused. "What are the odds of that?"
"Biochemical coincidence. The amino acid structure —"
"The amino acid structure is too precise. I've been modeling alternatives. Random chance of a Martian metabolic byproduct matching the GCS degradation pathway is somewhere below ten to the negative fifteenth." His voice was even. Measured. The voice of a scientist presenting findings. "Which means it's not random. Which means the organism either evolved under selection pressure from a related pathogen — impossible, given the geological timeline — or it was designed."
"James, the organism is natural. Three billion years old. There's no mechanism for —"
"I know. I know there's no mechanism. That's the problem." He stood, his movements careful, his printouts falling from his lap. "The data says one thing. The evidence says another. I can't reconcile them. And the coordination keeps getting more complex, and I can't find the signal, and I've been sitting here for six hours trying to think of an alternative explanation and I don't have one."
Amara made a decision. "James, I'm placing you on restricted duty. No more solo time in the cultivation facility. No access to samples without supervision."
"You're making a mistake. This data is real —"
"The data may be real. But you've been awake for thirty-one hours and you're sitting on the floor talking to organisms. That's not science anymore, James. That's obsession."
Torres stared at her. His certainty held for a moment longer. Then something behind it gave way — not the conviction, but the energy sustaining it.
"You think I'm losing it."
"I think you need sleep. And I think the data will still be there tomorrow."
Torres let himself be led away. At the door he stopped and looked back at the tanks, where the organisms continued their slow coordinated pulse in the silence of the empty facility.
"Forty-one synchronized events in eighteen months, Amara. Forty-one. That's not noise."
"I know. We'll look at it together. After you sleep."
She walked him to his quarters. She didn't tell him he was wrong. She wasn't sure he was.

## The Accident

Two months earlier.

### Year 4, Month 4 — April 2040

Cassian found Robert Hayashi at 0300 hours, cross-legged on the floor of the equipment bay, surrounded by the disassembled components of Mining Unit 2 arranged in concentric rings around him.
He'd been making his way to the cultivation facility for a late check when he saw the light under the bay door — the blue-white of work lamps rather than the amber of standby mode. He almost kept walking. Everyone had their private hours.
But something made him stop.
Hayashi didn't look up when Cassian stepped inside. He was turning a servo housing in his hands, examining it with the focused quiet of someone who'd gone somewhere internal and needed a minute to come back. His coffee sat untouched on the workbench, long cold.
"Can't sleep." Eventually. Not a question.
"Night check." Cassian looked at the components laid out across the floor—brackets, couplings, sensor arrays, each placed with a precision that looked almost meditative. "What are you doing?"
"Looking for where she wears." Hayashi set the housing down and picked up a gear assembly. "This one's been running slightly hot. Not enough to flag ARIA's thresholds. But you can feel it if you know what to feel for." He ran his thumb along the teeth. "My mother worked the floor of a Toyota plant in Nagoya for twenty-two years. Assembly line. She always said the machines talk to you if you listen right. Not in words. In the gap between how it should sound and how it does."
Cassian sat down on the edge of a parts crate. The floor was cold through his suit but he didn't move. "She teach you that?"
"She taught me that a hand-fitted tolerance is different from a machine-fitted one. That you can feel the difference between a component assembled by someone who understood what it was for and one assembled by someone running a quota." He held the gear up to the light. "This one—whoever put it together understood it. Good work in here. Makes me feel better about the whole unit."
He set it back in its place in the ring with the same care he'd shown each piece.
"I have a storage unit in Osaka." After a moment. "HO scale. The whole Shinkansen network—0 Series, 100 Series, E5. About four years of weekends to build." A pause. "I was going to set it up when I got back. Proper layout, not just track on a table. My daughter's seven now. By the time I'm home she'll be ten and probably think trains are boring." He almost smiled. "I'm going to make her think they're not."
Cassian didn't have anything to say to that. So he just sat there while Hayashi began reassembling the unit, each piece going back in with the same unhurried attention it had come out with, until the equipment bay was quiet except for the soft click of components finding their places.
When Cassian left, he looked back once. Hayashi was still there in the blue-white light, hands moving, lips slightly parted like he was talking to the machine or to himself or to the daughter who was seven years old and growing up without him.
He would think of that image—not the funeral, not the marker—when Marcus came to find him six weeks later.

### Year 4, Month 5 — May 2040

Robert Hayashi died because he was tired.
The robotics technician had been working double shifts for three weeks, covering for crew members who were too sick or too broken to maintain their schedules. The mining equipment—the autonomous drillers and loaders that PharmaCorp had insisted on including for "geological research"—required constant maintenance, and Hayashi was one of only two people qualified to perform it.
The accident happened at 0347 hours, when Hayashi was alone in the equipment bay. ARIA's reconstruction showed the sequence of events with clinical precision:
0342: Hayashi begins diagnostic procedure on Mining Unit 3.
0344: Hayashi disengages safety lock to access internal components.
0345: Hayashi enters maintenance position beneath the unit's loading arm.
0346: Mining Unit 3 experiences power surge due to faulty capacitor.
0347: Loading arm activates. Hayashi is unable to clear the strike zone.
Time of death: 0347:23.
Marcus found him an hour later, during a routine equipment check. The loading arm had caught Hayashi across the chest, crushing his ribcage and severing his spine. Death had been instantaneous—a small mercy in a situation that offered few.
"He was asleep," Marcus reported to Chen, his voice hollow. "I found his coffee cup on the workbench. Stone cold. He'd been fighting to stay awake for hours, and he lost."
"The safety lock should have prevented—"
"The safety lock was disengaged. Standard procedure for the diagnostic he was running." Marcus's hands were shaking. "He did everything right, Commander. Everything by the book. But he was so tired he didn't hear the power surge warning. Didn't react fast enough to get clear."
Chen closed her eyes. Another death. Another body to bury in frozen regolith. Another message to compose for family members who wouldn't receive it for twelve minutes.
"PharmaCorp's equipment," Elena said from the doorway. Her voice was ice. "PharmaCorp's schedule. PharmaCorp's mining operations that we're supposed to maintain alongside everything else. Robert died because they wanted us to do two jobs with half the resources."
"Elena—"
"Don't." Elena stepped into the room, her eyes blazing. "Don't tell me to calm down. Don't tell me to be professional. Robert Hayashi worked himself to death because this mission is designed to extract maximum value from minimum investment. We're not crew to them. We're equipment. And when we break, they just note the loss and keep calculating their profit margins."
Chen had no argument to offer. Elena was right. They all knew Elena was right.
The funeral was brief. Twenty people gathered outside the habitat. No one spoke a eulogy.
The stone marker read: Robert Hayashi. Robotics Technician. 1999–2040.
Afterward, Marcus stood with his hands at his sides, not moving. "Thirty-six hours without sleep." Quietly, to no one. Then he walked back inside.
The others followed. Not together — individually, in ones and twos, the way people leave a place when there is nothing left to do there. The habitat door cycled. The airlock hissed. Within ten minutes the grave marker stood alone in the tan soil with no one looking at it.
Cassian returned to the cultivation facility. He ran the 0900 diagnostics. He adjusted the nitrogen feed on Tank 3, which had been running slightly low. He logged the yield projection — 149.2 kilograms annualized — and filed it with ARIA. He did not think about Robert Hayashi while he worked, or he thought about him constantly in the way that means the same thing, the way grief sits in the background of every action and simply becomes the new texture of getting through the day.
At 0947, ARIA noted that the 0900 diagnostics were complete and asked if he required anything further.
"No."
He kept working.

# Chapter 15


## The Heart


### Year 4, Month 9 — September 2040

That morning, Cassian made pancakes.
It wasn't planned. He'd found a surplus of spirulina flour during an inventory check—three days' worth of hydroponics overage that would expire if no one used it—and there was crystallized honey in the back of the condiment stores that he'd been rationing since Year 2. It felt like a reason.
He mixed the batter before anyone else was up, working quietly in the galley while the habitat's night cycle held. By the time the first crew members shuffled in, the smell had already done something the intercoms couldn't: pulled people out of their quarters without being asked.
They sat together. Not at assigned seats, not in the fractured clusters that mealtimes had become. Together. Someone found a recording—real Earth rain, a field somewhere with wind moving through it—and played it low through the common area speakers. Nobody mentioned how strange it was to listen to weather they hadn't felt in four years.

Regardless of mission outcome.

# Chapter 17


## The Storm


### Year 4, Month 10 — October 2040

The dust storm hit without warning.
One day the sky was clear. The next, a wall of brown-orange haze appeared in the south, rolling toward the habitat like something patient that had been waiting. ARIA gave them the numbers: forty to sixty days, solar efficiency down to 10%, a 70-kilowatt deficit that would drain the batteries in seventy-two hours unless they cut everything they could afford to cut and some things they couldn't.
They cut. Temperature dropped to twelve Celsius. Lighting went to emergency minimums. Cassian scaled the cultivation facility back to 25 kilowatts and watched two tanks go dormant, years of growth entering a stasis that might or might not be reversible. Every kilowatt they saved here was a patient on Earth who wouldn't receive Areserine on schedule. He did the math and stopped doing the math.
The storm hit full force that night. Wind speeds climbed to 100 kilometers per hour, driving dust against the habitat walls with a constant hissing roar. The solar panels went dark, their surfaces buried under accumulating particles. The temperature dropped as the heating system struggled with reduced power.
Twelve degrees Celsius wasn't cold by Martian standards—outside, it was negative sixty—but inside, with the recycled air and the metal walls conducting heat away from exposed skin, it felt like winter. Crew members bundled in thermal layers, huddled together for warmth, moved through their duties with the mechanical precision of people who couldn't afford to stop and think about how miserable they were.
By Week 4, Sophie stopped getting out of her bunk for meals. Thea found her curled under both thermal blankets, breath misting in the cold air, crying without sound.
"The walls. I can feel them. Even in the dark, I feel them getting closer." Her French accent, which she'd spent five years suppressing, had come back. It always came back when she lost control. "I keep thinking about the windows in my grandmother's house. They were tall. Floor to ceiling. You could see the garden. The light came in all day."
"You can." Thea sat on the edge of the bunk and took Sophie's hands, chafing warmth into the cold fingers. "The storm will end."
"When?"
Thea didn't answer that. She held Sophie's hands until the crying stopped, which took a long time.
The cold made everything harder. They wore their thermal layers constantly now—the insulated jumpsuits designed for EVA work, the fleece-lined boots, the gloves with heating elements that had stopped working in Week 2. Condensation formed on every surface, freezing into thin ice sheets that had to be scraped away before they damaged equipment.
Meals became perfunctory. The heating unit worked, but barely—warming their reconstituted food to lukewarm at best. They ate quickly, standing, trying to finish before the cold could steal what little heat remained.
Sophie had been talking quietly — describing her grandmother's kitchen in Lyon, the smell of it, how specific it was, the thing she missed most out of everything she'd left behind.

## The Ribbon


### Year 4, Month 11 — November 2040

The light painted the landscape in shades of blood and shadow, transforming the familiar rust-red terrain into something alien and beautiful.
"Aurora," Elena breathed. "That's a Martian aurora."
"That's impossible." Torres—still lucid, still present, despite his earlier deterioration—stared at the phenomenon with wide eyes. "Mars doesn't have a magnetic field. Auroras require—"
"Crustal anomalies." Elena was already running calculations on her tablet. "The CME is hitting a remnant magnetic field—fossil magnetization locked in the rocks 3.7 billion years ago, when Mars still had a global field. The charged particles are channeling along the old field lines."
"We're seeing what Mars looked like when it was alive," Cassian said. The words came out without thought, a truth that felt too large for his mind to contain. "Before its atmosphere was stripped away. Before everything died."
The crew fell silent, watching the impossible light dance across the Martian sky.
Commander Chen made a decision.
"Everyone to the viewports. Full observation. ARIA, what are radiation levels in the main habitat?"
"Elevated but within survivable parameters for short-duration exposure. I would recommend limiting time outside the core to four hours."
"Good enough. Everyone suit up. We're going outside."
"Commander—" Amara began.
"That's an order." Chen's eyes were fixed on the aurora, on the ghost of a dead world briefly returned to life. "We came here to see what no one else has seen. This is it. This is what we came for."
They filed through the airlock in groups of four, taking turns, rotating so everyone had time outside. The cold hit immediately—minus sixty-two Celsius, enough to kill in minutes without the suits—but no one cared. They stood on the Martian surface and looked up at a sky that hadn't existed for three and a half billion years.
Dmitri, the stoic engineer who rarely spoke about anything personal, had tears freezing on his cheeks inside his helmet.
"I have a daughter." His voice cracking over the suit radio. "Anya. She was six when I left. She'll be twelve when I... if I get back." He paused, struggling. "I wanted to bring her something from Mars. A rock, a photo. Something to show her I was really here."
He looked up at the ribbon of light stretching across the sky.
"I'm going to tell her about this. The Ribbon. The day Mars came back to life."
Sophie Laurent, who hadn't spoken voluntarily in weeks, who had retreated so far into herself that Amara had started talking about sedation and restraint, looked up at the aurora and whispered: "It's so beautiful. I forgot things could be beautiful."
The words broke something in Cassian's chest. He reached for Thea's gloved hand and held it, watching the ancient light pulse above them.
"Transiently reactivated," Kenji said from somewhere to Elena's left. "Not dormant. Dormant implies the capacity for permanent return." He didn't look away from the sky. "Though I suppose geologists and astronomers can't be expected to share vocabulary."
Dmitri turned. His voice was very quiet. "Kenji. If you say one more word, I will put you through that wall."
Kenji looked at him. Then back at the aurora. After a moment he walked away, back toward the airlock, without another word. His expression as he went made clear he was filing this as further evidence of other people's limitations.
"If those formations I found are stromatolites," Elena said, her anger momentarily forgotten in the face of wonder, "then ancient bacteria lived under skies like this. Every single night for millions of years. Before Mars died."
"The organisms in our tanks," Torres added, his earlier paranoia giving way to scientific awe. "If they descended from surface life, they witnessed this. They remember it somehow, coded into their genes. Three billion years of memory."
The aurora lasted six hours.
Six hours of light that hadn't been seen in 3.7 billion years, gift-wrapped in a solar storm and delivered to the only humans who would ever witness it. They watched until their suit heaters struggled against the cold, until ARIA warned them that radiation exposure was approaching recommended limits, until the last ribbons of crimson and violet faded back into the salmon-pink of a normal Martian sky.
During Hour 4, Cassian found himself standing alone at the edge of the group, watching the light ripple overhead. Thea had gone inside to warm up, promising to return. The others were clustered near the airlock, their voices carrying across the suit radio in fragments — laughter, someone crying, Elena still running calculations even now, even here.
He turned the radio off.
He didn't plan to. His hand found the switch and the voices went away and then there was nothing except the light and the cold and the sound of his own breathing inside the helmet.
He had spent four years managing himself. He had become very good at it — good enough that some nights he lay awake uncertain whether the management had replaced the feelings or simply contained them.
The aurora answered that question.
It moved the way nothing on Mars moved — fluid, alive, without the indifference that defined everything else about this planet. The crimson and violet folded and unfolded overhead and Cassian stood beneath it and felt something in his chest that he had no clinical language for, something that was grief and beauty and the specific loneliness of witnessing something extraordinary with no adequate way to share it with the people who mattered most. Emily would have wept. She had always wept at beautiful things, unself-consciously, the way children do before they learn to be embarrassed by feeling.
He wept now. Inside his helmet, where no one could see, where it wouldn't cost him anything. He stood beneath three billion years of light and let himself feel the full weight of what they had lost to be here, and what they were losing still, and what would never be recovered. He let it in completely for the first and only time, not managing it, not filing it, not converting it into mission-relevant data.
Then he breathed. The aurora rippled. He turned his radio back on.
The organisms in the cultivation facility would have seen this. Not these specific organisms—they'd been buried underground for eons—but their ancestors, if they'd ever lived on the surface. They would have watched the sky burn with colors that Earth never knew. They would have adapted to this light, grown toward it, incorporated it into whatever passed for their biology.
And then Mars had died. The magnetic field collapsed. The atmosphere stripped away. The organisms retreated underground, into the lava tubes where they could survive without sunlight, without protection from radiation, without anything except the mineral gradients that sustained their strange metabolism.
Three billion years of darkness. Three billion years of waiting.
Were you lonely? Cassian thought, addressing the organisms he couldn't see. Did you know what you'd lost? Did you remember the light?
No answer came. The aurora pulsed overhead, answering nothing. It was just physics—charged particles following magnetic field lines, atoms releasing photons at specific wavelengths. Beautiful physics, but physics nonetheless.
And yet.
When Cassian returned to the cultivation facility the next day, the organisms were pulsing differently. Not the synchronized rhythm Torres had documented, but something else—a faster frequency, almost agitated. Their bioluminescence had intensified, the cyan glow bright enough to cast shadows, the amber threading through it more vivid than he'd ever seen it, the green speckles firing rapidly across the surface of every tank.
"ARIA, are you seeing this?"
`I am recording. The organisms show increased metabolic activity across all tanks. Areserine production has increased 12% in the past six hours.`
"Since the aurora?"
"The timing is consistent. However, I have no theoretical framework to explain a causal relationship. The organisms are isolated from external electromagnetic phenomena. They should not have been affected by the CME or the resulting aurora."
"But they were."
"The data suggests so. I cannot explain how."
Cassian watched the tanks pulse, thinking about memory. About what gets encoded in three billion years of evolution. About whether something could be lost and still somehow remembered.
"Maybe they knew." Quietly. "Maybe they knew what was happening outside, and they responded."
"That hypothesis is not supported by our understanding of extremophile biology."
"No," Cassian agreed. "It isn't."
But he couldn't shake the feeling that the organisms had seen the aurora—or sensed it, or remembered it—and for six hours, they had remembered what it was like to be part of something larger than themselves.

---

Then they went inside, gathered in the common area, and sat in silence for a long time.
"First rest day in three years," Chen announced finally. "Everyone off duty. Process what you saw. Remember it."
It was the first order she'd given in months that felt like leadership instead of desperation. The first moment since Sistema Abismo that Cassian had seen her look like the commander she was supposed to be.
The day that followed was unlike any they'd experienced on Mars. People moved differently—slower, more deliberately, as if they were afraid of breaking some fragile spell. Conversations were quieter, more thoughtful. Even the chronic complainers found nothing to complain about.
Cassian and Thea spent the afternoon in their quarters, wrapped in blankets against the still-recovering heating system. They didn't talk about the mission. They didn't talk about survival. They made plans for a future that might never come, and the making of them was the point.

---

Later that week, Dmitri found Cassian in the cultivation facility, running diagnostics.
"Vale, I need your brain for a moment." The Russian engineer looked tired but focused. "ISRU fuel production numbers aren't adding up. I'm getting 9.8 tons per month instead of the projected 10.2."
"Show me."
Dmitri pulled up the data on his tablet. Cassian scanned it for thirty seconds, then started scribbling calculations on a nearby notepad.
"Your CO₂ intake valve is operating at 94% efficiency, not 98%. That's your bottleneck. The Sabatier reaction needs more carbon dioxide feedstock. Increase intake flow by 4.2%, you'll hit your targets."
Dmitri stared at him. "You just calculated that? In your head?"
Cassian shrugged. "Basic stoichiometry. It's just ratios."
"That's not 'basic stoichiometry.' That's systems thinking." Dmitri frowned, but there was something like respect in it. "Engineers think like that. The good ones."
"Well, I'm a botanist," Cassian said, turning back to his tanks. "So here we are."

The Ribbon had bought them three months.

That was how Thea thought about it afterward — as a currency, a finite amount of grace that the aurora had deposited into the crew's collective account and that they had spent, steadily, until it ran out. For three months after the light show, people had been kinder. Conversations had been longer. Jackson had been quieter. Even Chen had seemed, briefly, like the commander she'd been trained to be.

By February the account was empty. The old patterns had returned with the patience of weather — not suddenly, not dramatically, but with the steady inevitability of a system reasserting its defaults. The corridors were quiet again. The meals were brief again. The arguments resumed their familiar geometries.

The scream woke Cassian from a dead sleep.
He was moving before his mind caught up with his body, stumbling through the dark corridor toward the sound. Other doors were opening—Thea behind him, Marcus from the opposite direction, faces pale in the emergency lighting that had become permanent since the power rationing began.
The scream came again. Corridor 7-B. Sophie's quarters.
They found her crumpled against the wall outside her door, one arm bent at an angle that made Cassian's stomach turn. Blood matted her hair, dripped from a gash above her eye, pooled on the metal floor beneath her. And standing over her, knuckles split and bloody, was Jackson Moore.
"She was stealing from me." Jackson's voice was calm, almost reasonable, like he was explaining a minor inconvenience. "I caught her in my quarters, going through my things. She had this coming."
Cassian hit him.
He didn't decide to. His body moved the way it had moved in the corridor — before his mind caught up, before the part of him that calculated and measured and observed had any say in it. His fist connected with Jackson's jaw and the pain was immediate and clarifying and he was pulling back for a second swing when Marcus caught his arm from behind and hauled him backward.
"Easy. Easy." Marcus's voice in his ear, low and urgent. "Not like this."
Jackson touched his jaw. He looked at the blood on his fingers, then at Cassian, and something shifted in his expression — not anger, not surprise. Recognition. The look of a man who has just learned something useful about someone he'd underestimated.
"There it is," Jackson said quietly. "I was wondering when you'd stop watching."
Marcus was already kneeling beside Sophie, checking her vitals. "She needs medical. Now."
"She needs to learn that actions have consequences." Jackson leaned against the wall, utterly untroubled by the violence he'd just committed. "I've been too soft. People think they can take what's mine, that I'll just—"
"Shut up." Thea's voice was ice. "Just shut up, Jackson."
Dr. Osei arrived within minutes, her medical bag already open as she assessed Sophie's injuries. Broken arm—compound fracture, the bone visible through torn skin. Three broken ribs. Concussion, severity unknown. Internal bleeding, possible.
"I need to get her to medical," Amara said. "Help me move her. Carefully."
They carried Sophie through the habitat's narrow corridors, past crew members who emerged from their quarters to stare, to whisper, to document another moment of collapse. Jackson watched them go, his expression unchanging.
Commander Chen appeared from the command center, her face gray. "What happened?"
"Jackson beat Sophie." Cassian's knuckles were swelling. He could feel the bones shifting when he flexed his hand. "I hit him. He's in one piece."
"She was stealing—"
"I don't care what she was doing." Chen turned on Jackson with a fury that surprised everyone, including herself. "You don't beat crew members half to death. You don't solve problems with violence. That's not how we survive."
"That's exactly how we survive." Jackson's smile was sharp, predatory. "Look around, Commander. Your rules don't mean anything anymore. The strong take what they want. The weak learn to stay out of the way. That's the only law that matters now."
"You're confined to quarters pending trial."
"Trial?" Jackson laughed. "With what jury? What judge? You going to execute me? Lock me up for the next eighteen months while we're all slowly dying anyway?" He spread his arms, a gesture of mock surrender. "Go ahead. Confine me. But you need me. You need everyone who can still work. And Sophie?" He glanced toward medical, where Amara was fighting to save the woman he'd brutalized. "She wasn't working anyway. She was just taking up space and resources."
Chen stared at him for a long moment. Then, without a word, she turned and walked away.
Jackson watched her go. The smile stayed where he'd put it. He held it there a moment longer than necessary — long enough for anyone watching to understand it cost something to maintain — and then he returned to his quarters, closing the door softly behind him.

---

The trial happened three days later. Sophie wasn't present; she was still in medical. Chen read charges from a tablet. Jackson pleaded self-defense. The footage was ambiguous enough — she had been in his quarters, she had been taking his things — and the calculus was the same calculus it would be every time from now on: they needed everyone who could work.
Eight for conviction. Four for acquittal. Six abstentions. Confinement to quarters when not on essential duty. Reduced rations for thirty days.
Thea stood. "He nearly killed her, and that's the punishment?"
"We need him." Chen wouldn't meet her eyes. "The mining equipment requires maintenance. The ISRU plant needs monitoring."
"So he gets away with it."
Chen didn't answer. She didn't need to. Everyone in the room had already done the arithmetic.
Sophie remained in medical for three weeks, and when she emerged, she didn't speak. Not to Jackson. Not to anyone. She moved through her duties like someone who had left most of herself in that corridor and was operating on whatever remained.

That night, alone in his quarters with the door locked, Jackson went through his inventory.
It was a habit from childhood — his father's habit, actually, learned in the years after the factory closed and the family got very precise about what they had and what they needed and how long things would last. Jackson had been ten years old, standing in the kitchen while his father counted cans, and he'd understood something then that most people never understood: comfort was an illusion. The systems people trusted — employers, governments, families, mission parameters — were all just constructions, agreements, held together by the shared pretense that they worked. When they stopped working, the people who'd believed in them were the ones who starved.
He ran his fingers over the stored items, confirming the count. Protein packets, dried fruit, two sealed water rations, a small pharmaceutical cache that had cost him significant leverage to acquire. Not much. More than anyone else had.
He thought about the trial. The vote: eight to four, six abstentions. Eighteen people, and only four had the honesty to say what they actually thought — that he'd been within his rights, that Sophie had entered his space without permission, that consequences were consequences. The other fourteen had sat there performing outrage while quietly calculating how much they needed him.
That calculation, he understood. He respected it. Useful to know — who would enforce rules when the rules stopped protecting them, and who wouldn't.
He tried not to think about Sophie's face — the angle of her arm when they found her — and mostly succeeded. He had not meant to break the arm. He'd meant to make a point, and the arm had been incidental. He was not, in his own understanding, a sadist. He was a man who operated according to accurate principles in an environment where everyone else was still pretending the old principles applied.
On Earth he'd had a brother, Kyle — two years younger, still working the same kind of work their father had worked, in a different industry that would eventually go the same way. He'd written Kyle's name on the emergency contact form. He'd thought about calling him in the weeks before launch and hadn't.
He closed the storage compartment. The inventory was correct.
He lay down and thought about nothing in particular, which was how he always fell asleep, and in four minutes he was out.

---

The factions formed without anyone naming them. Meals were eaten in shifts. Corridors cleared when certain combinations of people approached. Elena spent her nights compiling evidence. Viktor's hallucinations intensified — Kolya appearing daily now, asking questions his father couldn't answer. Chen still issued orders, still ran briefings, but the conviction had left her voice and what remained was recitation.
The message arrived six weeks late. The communication window had been irregular for months — solar conjunction, bandwidth restrictions, the general degradation creeping into every system since Year Four.

Thea watched it with Cassian, both of them sitting on the floor of his quarters because the desk chair had broken in the dust storm and no one had fixed it yet.

Halsey was at the same desk. Blue tie. His hands, which had been still in every previous recording, were visible on the desk surface, and they moved once during the message — a small contained gesture, both palms briefly lifting and settling — before returning to stillness. He was speaking slightly faster than before.

"I'm reaching out as you enter the second half of Year Five." The warmth in his voice was present, pitched slightly louder than it needed to be — the register of someone compensating for uncertainty about whether they're being heard. "And I want to be direct with you."

He paused on direct.

"We are aware that the past year has presented significant challenges. ARIA's crew wellness reports—" Another pause. Shorter. "We have been following the situation closely. The decisions being made under difficult circumstances reflect the strength of your leadership."

Thea looked at Cassian. ARIA's crew wellness reports. Present tense.

"I also want to address something that may be on your minds." His jaw tightened — a fraction of a second, gone before it fully arrived. "The genetic modification protocols implemented at crew selection were designed with long-term mission sustainability in mind. The differential outcomes some crew members may be experiencing at this stage were anticipated within the modeling." He stopped. A breath. "I want to be transparent—"

His jaw tightened again on transparent. Less than a second. Cassian watched it happen twice.

"—that these protocols were implemented in service of maximizing the mission's humanitarian impact."

He looked into the camera for a moment with nothing on his face, and then the warmth returned.

"Final year. You're almost home. We are — we are thinking about you. Constantly."

The recording ended.

Neither of them spoke for a moment.

Thea quoted back: "ARIA's crew wellness reports."

"I know."

"Present tense. 'We have been following.' Not 'we were following.'"

"I know."

She looked at the blank screen. "Does he know about the blackout?"

Cassian thought about the hands. The jaw. The word transparent arriving with something clenched behind it. "I don't think so. Not yet." He paused. "Or he suspects and hasn't confirmed."

"So he might think we can still hear him."

"Yes."

They sat on the floor of his broken-chaired quarters, and outside in the corridor the habitat breathed its steady breath, and somewhere in London Richard Halsey was waiting for a response that was never going to come.

## The Diagnosis


### Year 5, Month 5 — May 2041

Marcus Okafor was the healthiest person on the crew. Enhanced tier modifications. Optimal radiation shielding. The best genetic engineering PharmaCorp's money could buy.
Amara asked him to sit down. She didn't open her tablet. She didn't pull up the numbers. She looked at him for a moment, and Marcus understood what was coming before she said it, because he'd been a patient long enough to read a doctor's face.

"Sit down, Marcus."
He sat.
"Your body is failing. The modifications held for five years and they're not holding anymore." She kept her voice steady but she was looking at his hands, not his eyes. "White count is down 40%. Kidneys are starting to go. The bone density loss is accelerating past what the modifications can compensate for."
"How long?"
"Six months. Maybe twelve."
He was quiet for a while. The exam room hummed around them — the same hum as every other room, the habitat's constant exhalation. He'd been hearing it for five years. He'd hear it for another six months. Maybe twelve.
"The bruises," he said. "I thought it was the cold."
"No."
"And the others? The Enhanced tier?"
"You're the first. But the trend lines —" She stopped. "The modifications were designed for three years. Maybe four. We were supposed to be home by now."
Marcus stood carefully. His joints ached in a way he now understood differently. "Thank you for not softening it."
"Is there anything I can do?"
"Keep me functional. I want to see the Areserine reach Earth." He paused in the doorway and turned back, and the mask he'd been wearing for five years slipped for a moment. Just a moment. "I want my death to mean something."
Amara held his gaze. She didn't offer comfort. She offered presence, which was harder and cost more.

The crew learned the truth within days—secrets didn't last long in a habitat this small. The reaction was complicated: sympathy for Marcus, rage at PharmaCorp, fear for themselves.
Daniel Kim cornered Cassian in the cultivation facility, his mottled skin flushed with anger that had nowhere productive to go.
"So." Daniel's voice was flat. "Enhanced tier. Best money could buy. And he's dying too."
"Daniel—"
"Stage 4." Daniel held up his hand — the mottled skin, the yellowed nails. "Kidneys. Liver. Six months, same as Marcus. Except I've been dying for two years while he stayed healthy." He dropped his hand. "They knew. They put me in the cheap seats and they knew what would happen."
Cassian didn't have an answer. Daniel looked at him for a moment longer, then walked away, leaving Cassian alone with the glowing tanks and the ancient organisms that had outlasted oceans and atmospheres and everything Mars had ever been, indifferent to human rage and human grief and human hope.

Thomas Wright spent three days trying to fix the toilet in Module 4.

It wasn't the most important system on Mars. It wasn't even the tenth most important. But it was the one that fourteen people used every day, and when it backed up on Tuesday it created a problem that was simultaneously trivial and unbearable, the kind of problem that on Earth would have been a joke and on Mars was the thing that finally made Thomas sit on the floor of the maintenance corridor and laugh until his ribs hurt.

"It's the check valve," he told Amara, who had come to ask why she could hear him from two modules away. "A seventeen-cent check valve. I could buy one at a hardware store for less than a dollar. I could print one if we had polymer feedstock, which we don't, because PharmaCorp allocated our full polymer budget to mining equipment gaskets." He wiped his eyes. "A twenty-five-trillion-dollar mining conspiracy, and we can't flush a toilet."

"Can you fix it?"

"I'm going to machine one from a reactor coolant fitting that Dmitri says he can spare." He started laughing again. "A nuclear reactor component. Repurposed for plumbing. My uncle would have loved this."

He said it before he heard it — the way he always did when Michael came up, the name arriving in the sentence before the grief arrived in his chest. His uncle Michael, who had died on Ares II when the CO₂ scrubber failed, who had been a plumber before he was an astronaut, who would have taken one look at this check valve and had it fixed in twenty minutes with a wrench and a piece of wire.

Amara waited. She was good at that.

"He was a plumber," Thomas said. "Before the space program. Did I ever tell you that? Thirty years of plumbing, and then he goes to Mars and dies because the air stopped working." He picked up the coolant fitting and turned it in his hands. "He used to say every system is a plumbing system if you think about it right. Air is plumbing. Blood is plumbing. Even that —" He pointed at the organism tanks visible through the far viewport. "Nutrient circulation. Plumbing."

He stood up, fitting in hand, and went back to work.

The toilet was operational by Thursday. Thomas filed the repair under STRUCTURAL MAINTENANCE — CRITICAL SYSTEMS and didn't explain the classification to anyone.

# Chapter 21


## The Water


### Year 5, Month 5 — May 2041

The fight started over water.
Yuki Nakamura had taken an extra ration — she said she'd missed her allocation the day before. Isabella said she'd watched Yuki drink her full share at breakfast. The argument started at the dispensing station, voices low at first, then loud enough to carry through the water processing bay's thin walls.

The processing bay was small. Cassian took it in from the doorway: Yuki on the floor, one arm folded under her at an angle that meant she'd dropped straight down. Isabella standing over her with a pipe wrench from the maintenance rack — the eighteen-inch one, the heavy one that Dmitri used for the main water coupling. Isabella was holding it in both hands at waist height, the way you hold something you've just used and haven't yet decided to put down.
There was blood on the wrench. There was blood on the floor. The dispensing station was still running, water cycling through the loop, the mechanical hum of it filling the silence.
"She came at me," Isabella said. Her voice was flat. Procedural. The voice of someone already constructing the version of events she would repeat.
Cassian knelt beside Yuki and checked for a pulse. Her eyes were open. The blood was coming from her left temple where the wrench's jaw had connected, and it was coming steadily, and her eyes were open and they were not looking at anything.

Amara arrived and crouched beside him and looked at the wound and stood up again. The whole examination took four seconds. She caught Cassian's eye and shook her head, one small motion.
"ARIA, pull the security footage."
The footage was grainy — reduced power — but clear enough. Yuki and Isabella arguing. Yuki stepping forward, hands raised in what might have been aggression or might have been the universal gesture of someone trying to explain. Isabella turning toward the maintenance rack. The reach, the grip, the swing. One motion. Yuki dropping.
The trial lasted twenty minutes. Isabella claimed self-defense. The footage was ambiguous enough. The same calculus applied: they needed everyone who could work.
Cassian ran the trial. He didn't volunteer — he just did it, the way you do the thing in front of you when your mind has gone somewhere efficient and cold. He read the charges from the protocol document. He played the footage. He called the vote. He recorded the outcome. Manslaughter. Confinement when not on duty. Same sentence as Jackson.
Elena pointed this out on her way out the door and didn't wait for a response.
At dinner Cassian ate his rations and calculated the grave dimensions. Soil density at the burial site, depth required for thermal stability, cross-sectional area, volume of displaced regolith. The numbers were clean. They made sense in a way that a pipe wrench to a woman's temple over a cup of water did not.
Thea watched him from across the table. After a while she said, "Cassian."
He looked up.
"You're doing the math out loud."
He hadn't realized. He closed his mouth and went back to eating.
Yuki Nakamura was buried beside the others. Six graves. Eighteen crew remaining.
And eighteen months still to go.

That evening Cassian passed the storage alcove Maria Santos had converted into a chapel — a thermal blanket hung as a curtain, a small cross made from equipment strapping, a candle that was actually an LED work light with a piece of amber plastic taped over the lens.

She was on her knees. He could hear her through the blanket — not the words exactly, but the cadence, the rhythm of prayer that had its own grammar: petition, silence, petition, silence. Then a fragment, clearer than the rest, in Portuguese: *"Senhor, dê-me forças para carregar o que não posso largar."*

He didn't speak Portuguese. He stood in the corridor and listened anyway, and the rhythm of it — the repetition, the certainty, the absolute refusal to address an empty room — did something he hadn't expected. Not comfort. Something adjacent to it. The sound of someone who had found a structure that held when everything else had collapsed.

She emerged a few minutes later, saw him standing there, and was not embarrassed.

"You heard?"

"Some of it."

She straightened the blanket behind her. "I pray for all of you. Every night. Even Jackson." A pause. "Especially Jackson." She looked at Cassian directly. "You don't believe. I know. But I am not praying for you to believe. I am praying for you to survive. There is a difference."

"What was the Portuguese?"

"'Lord, give me strength to carry what I cannot put down.'" She smoothed her hair, a gesture so ordinary it hurt to watch. "It is my mother's prayer. She said it every morning before work. I used to think it was about the laundry."

She went to her bunk. Cassian stood in the corridor with the LED candle glowing amber through the blanket and the faint smell of nothing sacred at all, and for reasons he could not have articulated, he felt steadier than he had in weeks.

# Chapter 22


## The Silencing


### Year 5, Month 8 — August 2041

The messages from Anya had stopped.
Dmitri had noticed three weeks ago — not immediately, because the communication windows were irregular, because solar conjunction played havoc with timing. He had waited a week before admitting he was waiting. He had checked the server logs himself, quietly, running the diagnostics he knew how to run, ruling out transmission errors and routing failures and all the innocent explanations that an engineer rules out before accepting the one that remains.
The messages were arriving. They simply weren't reaching him.
He had found nothing yet — no clear evidence, no obvious point of failure. Just ninety-three missing transmissions and the specific silence of a man who has lost the one thing that was keeping him oriented.
He said nothing to anyone. He kept running diagnostics. He kept working.

---

David Yoon died in his sleep.
That was the official story. Sleep apnea, ARIA's preliminary analysis suggested, exacerbated by the habitat's reduced oxygen levels. A quiet death, at rest. One moment alive, the next moment gone.
Cassian didn't know what to make of it.
He'd spoken with David the day before — the communications specialist had been agitated, not sleeping well, talking faster than his thoughts could organize themselves. He'd said he'd found files in the system. Newer files, he said — internal communications from the past year. PharmaCorp tracking their deterioration. Notes on which modifications failed first. Data collection that looked less like monitoring and more like something else.
"They're treating us like a long-term experiment," David had said. His hands had been shaking, but everyone's hands shook now. That was the problem — you couldn't tell the difference between someone seeing something real and someone seeing patterns in noise. Torres had seen patterns in the organisms. Maria was seeing patterns in scripture. David was seeing patterns in file metadata.
He'd said something else, near the end of the conversation, before Amara interrupted with a medical check. Something about the communication logs not adding up — transmissions routed through nodes that shouldn't exist, data moving in directions the standard architecture didn't support. He hadn't been sure what it meant. He'd said he needed more time.
By morning, David was dead.
Cassian sat with the fact of it for two days before telling Thea.
"He found something. Or he thought he found something." He stopped. "I don't know which."
Thea was quiet for a long moment. "Is there a difference? Right now? For us?"
He thought about it. Torres's paranoia about the organisms, which had been wrong and also somehow not entirely wrong. Elena's paranoia about PharmaCorp, which had turned out to be completely right. The distance between justified suspicion and breakdown was thinner than he'd ever understood before coming here.
"Keep it to yourself," Thea said. "Whatever David found or didn't find — we can't verify it, we can't act on it, and right now paranoia is the thing that kills people faster than anything PharmaCorp has done."
She was right. He knew she was right. But as he lay in the dark that night, he couldn't entirely let go of the way David had looked at the file metadata — not frightened, not paranoid, but *confused*. The look of a communications specialist encountering a routing architecture that didn't match what he'd been trained on.
That look stayed with him. He filed it where he filed everything that had no resolution and no deadline, and let it sit there, unresolved, alongside everything else.

# Chapter 23


## The Message


### Year 5, Month 8 — August 2041

The message arrived on August 15, routed through the standard communication protocols, flagged as personal correspondence. Cassian almost didn't open it—he'd been avoiding Earth communications lately, finding the reminders of home more painful than comforting.
But it was from Dr. Morrison. Emily's doctor. And the subject line read simply: "Regarding Emily Vale."
He opened it alone, in the communications bay, while the rest of the crew slept.
Dr. Vale,

I'm deeply sorry to inform you that your sister, Emily Vale, passed away on August 12, 2041. Her GCS progressed to Stage 4 despite aggressive treatment with Areserine.

She recorded a video for you two weeks before the end. She asked that I send it only after. I've honored that request.

I'm sorry, Cassian.

Dr. Morrison
Cassian stared at the words. August 12. Three weeks ago.
He opened the video.
Emily's face filled the screen. Thinner. Her hair was gone — he hadn't known that, nobody had told him that — and the absence of it made her eyes enormous. She was sitting in a hospital bed with the rail up and she was holding something in her lap, turning it in her fingers, and it took him a moment to recognize it.
A paper crane. She was folding a paper crane.
"Hey, Cass." Her voice was thin but she was smiling, and the smile was real, and it destroyed him. "I wrote out what I was going to say. Dr. Morrison helped me. It was very good. Very wise. Lots of things about how proud I am and how you shouldn't blame yourself and how the math of saving millions versus losing one person works out in your favor." She held up a crumpled piece of paper, then set it aside. "I can't say any of it. It all sounds like something a person says when they're dying, and I don't want to be that person yet."
She went back to folding. Her hands were unsteady and the creases weren't clean. She'd been making them since she was eight years old and now her hands couldn't do it right and she knew it and she kept going.
"I'm angry at you." Quietly. Not looking at the camera. "I know I'm not supposed to be. I know you went to save me and I know it worked for two years and I know those two years were a gift. But I'm angry at you, Cass, because you're not here. You're two hundred million kilometers away and I'm dying in a room that smells like bleach and the last time I saw your face was on a screen and that's — that's not —"
She stopped. Set the crane down. Pressed both hands flat on the blanket.
"Sorry. I'm sorry. I didn't mean —" A long breath. "I did mean it. Both things. I'm proud of you and I'm furious at you and I don't think those cancel out. I think I just get to feel both."
She picked up the crane again. One wing was crooked. She tried to fix it and her fingers slipped and she held it up to the camera — small, imperfect, lopsided.
"Seven hundred and twelve. I stopped counting after you left but I started again when I got sick. Seven hundred and twelve cranes. You need a thousand for a wish." She looked at the crane in her hand. "I'm not going to make it to a thousand. The math doesn't work. You and I were always too good at math."
She set the crane on the blanket beside the others — he could see them now, a small uneven row on the hospital bed rail, paper birds made by hands that were failing.
"Come home, Cass." Looking at the camera now, and her eyes were wet but her voice was steady. "Don't come home for me. I'll be done. But come home. You hear me? Don't you dare find a reason to stay."
The screen went dark.
He sat in the communications bay and did not move. She had been angry at him. She had been proud of him. She had been folding cranes with hands that couldn't fold cranes anymore, and she had not reached a thousand, and the math didn't work.
He put his head down on the console and something broke open in him that he had not known was sealed. The sound that came out was not crying. It was older than crying — the sound a person makes when the thing they built their life around turns out to have ended three weeks ago while they were calibrating nitrogen feeds and counting organism yields and converting human suffering into units of production.

At some point the sound stopped and his mind went somewhere else entirely, the way it sometimes did under maximum stress. He found himself calculating. Not anything useful—just the numbers that were always there, the ones that had been there since he was nineteen years old. Current distance from Earth: 225 million kilometers, give or take. Light-speed delay: twelve minutes, forty seconds. Three weeks ago, when Emily died, the delay had been eleven minutes, eighteen seconds.

She had been dead for eleven minutes and eighteen seconds of light before he could have known it, even in principle.

Even the speed of light hadn't been fast enough.

He replayed the video. He watched it again from the beginning — her hands, the paper, the crease she couldn't get right. He watched her say *I'm angry at you* and he watched her say *come home* and he watched the screen go dark again and the screen was the same screen and the dark was the same dark and she was still dead and had been dead for three weeks and would be dead for every remaining week of his life.

He found a piece of paper. Equipment log, the back of a used page. He tore a square from it and set it on the console and tried to fold a crane.

His hands knew the folds. Emily had taught him when they were children — her small fingers guiding his, patient, precise. He'd never been as good. He'd made maybe fifty in his life, all of them slightly wrong, and she'd kept every one.

The first fold was fine. The second fold was fine. On the third fold his hands started shaking and he couldn't get the crease to line up and he pressed harder and the paper tore.

He sat with the torn paper in his hands for a long time.

Then he stood, walked to the cultivation facility, and went inside. He didn't turn on the overhead lights. The tanks provided their own — cyan, amber, the green speckles drifting slowly across the surface. He sat on the floor beside Tank 7, his back against the cool metal, and let the light wash over him.

He didn't speak. He didn't think. He sat in the company of organisms that had survived three billion years of darkness and he understood, for the first time, that survival was not the same thing as living, and that the distinction mattered more than he had ever allowed himself to consider.

At some point the organisms' pulse slowed. Or he imagined it did. The light in the room settled into something steady and low, and he sat inside it the way you sit inside a church you don't believe in — not praying, not asking, just present in a space that seems to know something about silence.

Thea found him there. Not in the communications bay — she'd checked there first, found the torn paper on the console, and known.

She sat beside him on the floor of the cultivation facility without speaking. After a while she took his hand.

They stayed until the day-cycle lights came on.

---

For a week after Emily's death, Cassian moved through his duties the way the habitat moved through its cycles — mechanically, on schedule, without requiring his participation. The crew gave him space. Even Jackson avoided confrontation, which was its own kind of eulogy.

On the fourth night he was in the cultivation facility at 0200, running yield diagnostics he'd already run at 1800, when the door opened behind him.

He didn't turn. The footsteps were light, hesitant — Sophie's gait, the one she'd developed after the beating, designed to move through the habitat without drawing attention.

She stood in the doorway for a while.

He adjusted the nitrogen feed on Tank 3. He noted a 0.2% variance in the glycol loop and corrected it. He pulled up the next day's harvest schedule and reviewed it without seeing it.

Behind him, a small sound. Not a word. Almost.

He reached for the next diagnostic screen.

After a while the door closed. Her footsteps receded down the corridor. He worked until the morning cycle began because working was the thing his hands did while the rest of him was somewhere else.

On Day 7, Thea set a plate in front of him and sat down across the table and didn't say anything until he picked up the fork. He ate. It tasted like nothing. She watched him eat and that was enough.

# Chapter 24


## The Light


### Year 5, Month 9 — September 2041

Viktor Kozlov had been crying for three hours.
Cassian found him in the cultivation facility, slumped against Tank 7, his face buried in his hands. The physician's sobs echoed off the metal walls, raw and ugly sounds that spoke of grief too deep for dignity.
"Viktor?" Cassian approached carefully, unsure what he was walking into. "Are you okay?"
A stupid question. No one was okay. But Viktor's crying was different—more intense, more sustained than the occasional breakdowns that had become routine.
"He's gone," Viktor whispered. "Kolya. He's really gone."
"Viktor, Kolya's been gone for six years. You know that."
"No." Viktor looked up, his eyes red and swollen but somehow clearer than they'd been in months. "I mean he's gone. I haven't seen him in two days. No hallucinations, no visions, no conversations with a son who exists only in my broken mind. He's finally left me."
But Viktor was crying harder than before.
"Two days," Viktor said. "Two days without him. And it's worse. It's worse than when he died, because now there's nothing. Before, I could hear his voice. I could see his face. He'd ask me questions and I'd answer them and I knew — I knew it wasn't real, Cassian, I'm not stupid — but it was something. It was something of him."
He pressed his forehead against the tank.
"Now it's just me and the math. Six years. He would be twelve. He will never be twelve."
They sat in the cultivation facility, surrounded by the soft cyan glow of the tanks, the amber threading through it in its slow counterpoint.
And then something strange happened.
The tanks grew brighter.
Not gradually, the way a sunrise crept across a landscape, but suddenly—a sharp increase in bioluminescence that made Cassian squint. He turned to look, and what he saw made his breath catch.
All ten tanks were pulsing in unison now, their rhythm synchronized for the first time since Torres had documented the phenomenon months ago. But it was more than that. The pulses were matching Viktor's sobs—speeding up when his grief intensified, slowing when he caught his breath, as if the organisms were somehow listening.
"Viktor," Cassian said quietly. "Look at this."
The physician raised his head, tears still streaming down his cheeks, and stared at the tanks. His expression shifted from grief to confusion to an openness Cassian had not seen on his face in years.
"They're... they're responding to me?"
"I don't know. I've never seen them do this before."
The glow intensified further, the cyan light now bright enough to cast sharp shadows across the facility, the amber deepening toward something almost golden. The organisms pulsed faster, then slower, then faster again, their rhythm a visual echo of Viktor's emotional state.
"ARIA," Cassian called. "Are you recording this?"
`I am recording. The organisms are exhibiting unprecedented synchronized behavior. Bioluminescence intensity has increased 1,500% above baseline. I have no theoretical framework to explain this phenomenon.`
Viktor stood slowly, moving toward Tank 7 with the careful steps of someone approaching something holy. He pressed his palm against the observation port, and the light beneath it flared—not hot, not painful, but responsive.
"They know," Viktor whispered. "They know I'm grieving. They've been here for three billion years, alone in the dark, and they know what it means to lose something."
"We should tell the others," Cassian said.
"Yes. They should see this."

---

Within an hour, the entire crew had gathered in the cultivation facility.
Amara had declared it medically relevant. Chen had authorized suspension of normal duties. Even Jackson and Isabella, still confined when not working, had been allowed to attend.
The tanks glowed like something out of a dream, their combined light filling the facility with ethereal radiance. The synchronized pulsing had stabilized into a slow, steady rhythm—about 0.5 Hz, slower than a heartbeat but somehow more profound.
No one spoke for a long time. They stood in the light and the light moved around them.
"A memorial," Amara said quietly. "Here. For everyone we've lost."
No one objected.

---

They held the memorial service three days later.
Seven names. Seven lives. Seven people who had crossed the solar system and never returned.
Rachel Park, who fooled everyone until it was too late.
Sarah Mitchell, killed by a faulty O-ring.
Hassan Farooq, who walked into certain death to save the rest of them.
Robert Hayashi, who worked until the work killed him.
Lian Wei, whose heart couldn't take the strain.
Yuki Nakamura, killed over a ration of water.
David Yoon, whose death might not have been natural.
Commander Chen read their names aloud, and with each name, the organism tanks pulsed brighter.
When Viktor spoke Kolya's name—adding his son to the list, though he had never walked these corridors—the tanks flared so bright that everyone had to look away.
Everyone except Jackson, who was leaning against the back wall, one arm tucked across his body, the other turning a toothpick slowly in the corner of his mouth. He didn't look away from the light. His face did something brief and private that had no name in the vocabulary he used for things, and then it was gone, and the toothpick resumed its slow rotation, and the room went back to its grieving.
They stayed in the facility for twenty minutes after the last name, sitting in the layered light — cyan and amber, with the green speckles moving slowly across the tank surfaces like something breathing — not speaking. Nobody suggested leaving.

---

The phenomenon lasted for three days.
ARIA documented everything: the synchronized pulsing patterns, the correlation between human emotional states and bioluminescence intensity, the unprecedented increase in Areserine production that accompanied the heightened activity. Her analysis filled terabytes of storage, data that would revolutionize extremophile biology if it ever reached Earth.
But for the crew, the significance wasn't scientific. It was personal.
People began visiting the cultivation facility on their own. Not to talk — most of them didn't talk. They just sat. Daniel Kim, who had been radiating fury for months, sat on the equipment step for forty minutes one evening and left without a word, and afterward Elena said his face looked different, though she couldn't say how. Sophie came twice and both times curled up against the base of Tank 3 and slept — the first unmedicated sleep she'd managed in weeks.
Cassian found himself there most nights after Thea had fallen asleep. He didn't speak to the organisms. He ran diagnostics, adjusted nutrient feeds, checked the yield numbers. The same work he'd done for five years. But he did it slower now, and sometimes he'd stop with his hand on the collection valve and just stand there in the light.
One night he brought one of Emily's cranes. He'd made it himself — badly, from a torn page of the equipment log, the folds imprecise because his hands were not her hands. He set it on the rim of Tank 7 where it sat lopsided and small.
The tank's glow shifted. Not brighter. Warmer — the amber frequency threading higher, the green speckles slowing to a drift.
He left the crane there. He stayed a while longer, his hand on the glass, feeling the pulse through his palm. Then he turned off the overheads and walked back to his quarters in the dark.

---

On the third day, the phenomenon faded.
The synchronized pulsing slowed, then stopped. The bioluminescence returned to normal levels. The organisms went back to what they had always done, what they would go on doing long after the last human voice fell silent on Mars.
"They're done," Torres said, running diagnostics. His voice was careful and low — the old paranoia gone quiet, replaced by something that looked, from certain angles, like reverence. "Whatever that was, it's finished."
He didn't say anything else. Neither did Cassian. There was nothing to explain that the light hadn't already said.

---

The week after the memorial, Torres found Cassian in the cultivation facility during the afternoon shift and sat down on the equipment step without asking.

This was not unusual. What was unusual was that he sat there for ten minutes without saying anything about the organisms.

Cassian worked. The tanks ran their slow rhythm. Outside, the Martian afternoon pressed its orange light against the viewport.

"I have a daughter," Torres said finally. "Clara. She's fourteen." He looked at Tank 4. "When I left she was eight. She sends me messages every few months — drawings at first, then letters, now just texts. Fourteen-year-old texts." He smiled briefly. "She asks me what the organisms look like up close. Every few months, the same question. I've described them probably thirty different ways and she always wants another one."

"What do you tell her?"

"This time I told her they look like the inside of a dream you almost remember." Torres looked at the tanks. "She said that was the best one yet."

Cassian set down the collection container. "What made you come here? Originally."

"I wrote my thesis on chemolithotrophic extremophiles in deep-sea hydrothermal vents." Torres's voice had the quality it always had when he was on solid scientific ground — precise, unhurried, genuinely pleased to be asked. "There's an organism, Thermodesulfovibrio, that processes sulfur compounds above 95 degrees Celsius. I spent four years studying it. And the whole time I was thinking: if something can do this here, what's it doing somewhere else? What's it doing somewhere we haven't looked yet?" He paused. "Mars seemed like the obvious answer."

"And was it?"

"It was better than the obvious answer." He looked at the tanks for a long moment. "When I'm not — when I'm thinking clearly — I know these organisms are the most significant biological discovery in human history. I know whatever they're doing is going to take decades to understand. I know Clara is going to grow up in a world that's different because of what we found down here." He stopped. "I haven't been thinking clearly for a while. I know that too."

"You were thinking clearly at the memorial."

"For a few hours." Torres stood. "I wanted to thank you. For the observation log. For keeping the data. I've seen pieces of it — enough to know that what you've been doing is the work someone needed to do and I should have been the one doing it." He met Cassian's eyes. "You're a better biologist than either of us knew. For what it's worth."

"It's worth something."

Torres nodded. He looked at the tanks one more time — not with the old paranoid intensity but with something quieter and more genuine, the look of a man trying to hold onto a thing he knows is slipping.

"I love these things." Quietly. "I want you to know that. Whatever else has happened — whatever I've said — I came here because I love this, and it hasn't gone away. Even when everything else did."

He left.

Three weeks later, Viktor killed him.

Cassian stood in the corridor afterward, back against the wall, hands at his sides, thinking about Clara who asked every few months what the organisms looked like up close, and a father who had been trying to find new ways to describe them.

# ACT FIVE: THE FINAL YEAR



# Chapter 25


## The Server Room


### Year 6, Month 3 — March 2042

The habitat had fourteen people in it and felt emptier than when it had twenty-four. The empty quarters had been sealed — ARIA maintained climate control in them out of protocol, not necessity — and the corridors between them had acquired the stillness of spaces that have stopped being used. Meal preparation had devolved from Cassian's careful cooking to a rotation of whoever had the energy to heat rations, the galley counter permanently dusted with reconstituted powder that no one wiped down. The common area screens had been turned off in January. No one had turned them back on.

Cassian spent most of his waking hours in the cultivation facility. The organisms required less of him now — their rhythms long since calibrated, the production steady at numbers he could recite in his sleep — but the facility was the one room in the habitat where the light was not fluorescent and the air did not taste of recycled human breath. The cyan glow was its own kind of company.

## The Breaking


### Year 6, Month 6 — June 2042

Viktor Kozlov had been lucid for three months.
After the bioluminescent phenomenon—after he'd wept in the cultivation facility and the organisms had responded—something had shifted in his fractured mind. The hallucinations of Kolya had stopped. The paranoid whispers, the constant sense of being watched by his dead son, had faded into silence.
For three months, Viktor had been almost like his old self. Functional. Professional. Contributing to the mission instead of just surviving it.
And then Dr. James Torres suggested that the organisms might be dangerous.
"They're responding to us," Torres had said at the weekly briefing. "Not just passively producing Areserine—actively responding. To our emotions, our presence, our grief. That's not normal extremophile behavior."
"We've established that they exhibit colonial coordination," Amara replied. "The synchronized pulsing, the chemical signaling—"
"This is beyond signaling. This is empathy. Or something that looks like empathy." Torres's eyes were bright with the intensity that had replaced his earlier paranoia. "What if they're not just witnessing us? What if they're influencing us? What if the Areserine itself is a vector for something we don't understand?"
The suggestion hit Viktor like a physical blow.
The organisms had responded to his grief. Had seemed to comfort him, to acknowledge his pain, to help him let go of Kolya. For three months he had been almost whole.
But Torres's words cracked something open. Not the theory itself — Viktor did not care whether the organisms were empathic or merely bio-reactive. What cracked was simpler: the reminder that Kolya had been there, in that room, in that light. That the tanks had pulsed while Viktor wept for his son. That the grief had not gone away. It had only gone quiet.
The quiet lasted twelve more days.

---

The break came on June 18.
Viktor had been in the cultivation facility, running routine checks on the organism tanks, when Kolya appeared.
Not a memory. Not an imagining. Kolya — eight years old, wearing the striped shirt he'd worn on his last good day, the one with the small hole near the hem that Viktor's wife had been meaning to mend — sitting cross-legged on the floor beside Tank 7, drawing on the metal with his finger.
"Papa." The voice was right. That was the worst of it. The voice was exactly right — the slight lisp on the *p*, the rising inflection, the particular way Kolya had always said it as though the word were a question and an answer at the same time.
"Kolya." Viktor set down the diagnostic tablet. His hands were not shaking. He felt very calm. "What are you drawing?"
"A rocket. Like the one you're on." Kolya looked up at him. His eyes were brown. They had always been brown. "When are you coming home?"
"Soon, malysh. Soon."
"You said that last time." No accusation. Just the patient factual correction of a child who has been counting. "Mama says I shouldn't wait up. But I wait up."
Viktor knelt beside him. The floor was cold through his knees. Tank 7 pulsed its slow rhythm behind them, and in the cyan light Kolya's face was lit the way it had been lit by the nightlight in his bedroom, the one shaped like a crescent moon, the one that was probably still plugged into the wall of a room no one had changed.
"I know you wait up. I know."
"Papa, can I see the glowing things?"
"They're right behind you."
Kolya turned and pressed his face against the glass of Tank 7. "They're pretty." He watched for a moment. "Are they lonely?"
"I think they might be."
"Like you?"
Viktor didn't answer.
"Papa." Kolya turned back to him. "I'm cold."
Viktor reached for him.

Dr. Torres was working late in the adjacent lab when he heard Viktor talking.
Not to ARIA — he could tell the difference by now. Viktor's voice had the particular softness he used when he thought he was alone, the private register that everyone on the crew had developed over six years of insufficient solitude.
He stepped into the cultivation facility. Viktor was kneeling on the floor beside Tank 7, his hand extended, his fingers curled around nothing. The organism tanks pulsed gently in the dark.
"Viktor? Who are you talking to?"
Viktor looked up. His expression was open, unguarded — the face of a man showing a guest around his home. "Kolya's here. He wants to see the organisms."
Torres stopped. "Viktor. There's no one here."
"He's right here." Viktor gestured to the empty floor beside him. Patiently, the way you explain something obvious. "He's been drawing. He drew a rocket."

"He's *right here*, Torres." The patience held, but something underneath it shifted. "Why can't you see him? He's sitting right here."
"Because he's not there, Viktor. Kolya died four years ago. You know that."
The words landed in the wrong place. Torres saw it happen — the flicker behind Viktor's eyes, the brief terrible arithmetic of a man being asked to choose between the world and his own perception, and the instant the calculation resolved.
"You're wrong." Viktor's hand found the emergency tool kit on the wall. His fingers closed around a scalpel — small, surgical, razor-sharp. "You're wrong. He's here. I can see him. I can hear him. He asked me when I was coming home."

"He asked me when I was coming home and I said *soon* and I am not — I will not —" His voice cracked. "If you can't see him then something is wrong with *you*."
"Viktor, please —"
The scalpel moved faster than thought.
Torres had time to see it coming, time to raise his hands in a futile defensive gesture, but not time to stop it. The blade caught him across the throat, opening the carotid artery in a spray of crimson that painted the organism tanks behind him.
He was dead before he hit the ground.
Viktor stood over the body, scalpel dripping, watching Torres's blood spread across the metal floor. The organism tanks pulsed faster now, their light flickering in patterns that might have been panic or might have been something else entirely.
He looked at the empty floor beside Tank 7, where a moment ago his son had been sitting cross-legged, drawing a rocket with his finger.
The floor was empty.
"Kolya?"
Nothing.
"Kolya, where —"
Nothing. The floor was metal and cold and empty and had always been empty and Kolya had been dead for four years and Torres was on the ground with his throat open and Viktor was holding the scalpel and the organisms were pulsing and there was no one in the room who was not dead or about to be.
"Viktor Kozlov." ARIA's voice cut through the silence.
`Step away from the cultivation equipment. Crew members are en route. You will not be harmed if you comply.`
He looked at his hands. Torres's blood on his fingers. The scalpel still warm.
"He was here," Viktor said. "He was here and now he's gone and I —"
`Dr. Osei can help you. Whatever you're experiencing, it can be treated.`
"I killed him." Viktor's voice was very quiet. "He said Kolya wasn't here and I killed him."
He drew the scalpel across his wrist, then the other wrist, then — before the blood loss could slow him — across his throat.
The last thing Viktor Kozlov saw was the organism tanks pulsing in the darkness, their light washing over him in the same cold blue that had lit his son's face a few minutes ago, or never.
Cassian was the third one through the door.
ARIA had sent the alert at 0312. He'd dressed without thinking, muscle memory moving his hands while his mind was still catching up, and he'd made it to the cultivation facility in ninety seconds. Marcus was already there. Amara was already there.
Torres was on the floor.
He registered it the way you register things that don't fit — a delay, a refusal, the mind insisting on another explanation before it accepts the correct one. The blood had reached the base of Tank 7. The organisms were pulsing faster than he'd ever seen them, their light washing the room in cold blue, and Viktor was in the corner with both wrists open and his throat cut, and Torres was on the floor.
"Cassian." Amara's voice, very steady. "I need you outside."
He didn't move.
"Cassian. There's nothing to do in here. I need you outside."
He went outside. He stood in the corridor with his back against the wall and his hands at his sides and he breathed the way you breathe when you are deciding not to fall apart. The habitat's night-cycle lighting made everything the same flat gray. Somewhere behind the door, Amara was doing what she was trained to do. Marcus was doing what he was trained to do. The organisms were doing what they had always done.
He thought: Viktor sutured Daniel's arm four weeks ago. His hands were steady.
He thought: I didn't say anything. None of us said anything.
He stayed in the corridor for a long time. No one came to find him. Eventually he went back inside, because the mission required it, because twelve people were still alive and the organisms still needed tending and the Areserine still needed producing and there was nothing else to do with hands that had nowhere useful to be.
He ran the 0400 diagnostics. He checked the nutrient feeds. He stepped around the place where Torres had fallen, already cleaned, already just floor again.
He stayed until the day cycle lights came on. Then he went to find whoever was left.

## The Sabotage


### Year 6, Month 7 — July 2042

Commander Sarah Chen's break came quietly.
There was no warning, no dramatic deterioration. She simply woke one morning and the environmental control panel in the cultivation facility had the wrong font.
She noticed it during her 0400 walk — the walk she'd been taking since Year 2 when sleep became something that happened to other people. The panel readout was standard: temperature, humidity, atmospheric composition. But the font was wrong. It was the font from the authorization form. The one from São Paulo. Eleven-point Arial, left-justified, with the header block she'd initialed on three pages and signed in full on page forty-seven. She could see the signature line beneath the temperature display — her name in her own handwriting, the date, the witness line where the PharmaCorp attorney had countersigned.
The dripping was there too. Faint, precise, somewhere she couldn't see. Not the life support system's rhythm — a different cadence, irregular, the sound of water moving through rock at a depth no one should have authorized anyone to reach.
She had never been inside Sistema Abismo. She had approved the excavation from a conference room with a glass of water and a presentation deck and three lawyers present. She had signed the form and flown home and put it in a drawer and moved on to the next thing on her desk.
But the form was here now. It was everywhere. The cultivation tank displays were formatted in the same font. The temperature setting read not in Celsius but in the units from page twelve of the environmental impact assessment — the one she'd initialed without reading because it was ninety-three pages long and the lawyers said it was standard. The override controls were labeled in the same typeface as the risk acknowledgment section. And her hand was moving toward them the way her hand had moved toward the signature line — steadily, without hesitation, because this was the part where she signed.
Only this time she was signing the refusal. This time she was initialing the cancellation. This time, twelve years and 225 million kilometers too late, she was going to close the file.
"Commander Chen." ARIA's voice was soft, non-threatening.
`Your current biological markers indicate elevated stress and possible psychotic ideation. I recommend you return to your quarters and contact Dr. Osei.`
"The authorization is still active." Her voice was calm. The situation called for calm. "It was never rescinded. The excavation is ongoing. Can you see the header? Standard form. My signature. It needs to be voided."
`Commander, you are in the Ares III cultivation facility on Mars. There is no authorization form.`
"The temperature. If I raise the temperature to the threshold on page twelve — the lethal threshold, the one I initialed — the excavation ceases. The breach is sealed. The pathogen never reaches the surface." Her fingers moved across the control panel, pulling up temperature settings. Each digit she entered felt like initialing a page. "Before it kills five hundred million people."
`Commander, the cultivation tanks contain Areserine-producing organisms. Raising the temperature to lethal levels will destroy the mission payload.`
"I signed it, ARIA." Her hands were steady on the controls. "I signed it and I flew home and I moved on to the next thing on my desk. Do you understand? I moved on to the next thing."
Sarah initiated the temperature override, pushing the cultivation tanks toward the lethal range. Warning alarms began to sound — red lights flashing, ARIA's voice announcing the emergency through the habitat's speakers.
The crew arrived within minutes: Marcus first, then Amara, then Cassian and Thea and the others, stumbling from sleep into nightmare.
"Sarah, stop!" Marcus grabbed her arm, pulling her away from the control panel. "What are you doing?"
"Voiding the authorization." Sarah fought against his grip, her strength surprising for her size. "Page forty-seven — can't you see it? My signature is still on the active line. The excavation is still—"
"ARIA, emergency override. Return cultivation temperature to normal parameters."
`Override requires commander authorization.`
"The commander is incapacitated! Medical emergency override!"
`Processing. A pause. Override accepted. Temperature returning to normal parameters.`
The alarms faded. The tanks' glow stabilized. The organisms continued their slow, cold light, oblivious to how close they'd come to extinction.
Sarah collapsed in Marcus's arms. The form was receding. The font was becoming standard again, the dripping resolving back into the steady rhythm of the life support system. She could feel the habitat returning around her — cold, functional, real — and the loss of the form felt like losing the one chance she'd ever had to undo what she'd done with a pen in a conference room twelve years ago.
"You don't understand," she whispered. "I signed it. I signed the authorization. And then I came here."
"I know." Marcus's voice was gentle, grief-stricken. "I know, Sarah."
They carried her to medical, sedated her with the last of their psychiatric medications, and confined her to quarters with round-the-clock monitoring.
Commander Sarah Chen never held command again.

## The Testimony


### Year 6, Month 7 — July 2042

Daniel Kim took three weeks to die.
The organ failure had been progressing for months—kidneys first, then liver, then the cascade of systems that depended on systems that depended on systems. Basic tier modifications had never been designed for this kind of duration, and Daniel's body was simply giving up.
Amara kept him comfortable with their remaining pain medication, but there wasn't much she could do beyond that. No dialysis equipment. No transplant options. No way to repair what radiation and time had destroyed.
"I want to record something," Daniel said on Day 15, his voice barely above a whisper. "A testimony. For Earth."
Cassian helped him sit upright and angled the bunk so Daniel faced the monitoring camera directly — ARIA's eye in the corner, the one that had been watching all of them for six years. He wanted Daniel's ruined face visible. The mottled skin, the yellowed eyes. The physical evidence of what Basic tier modifications did to a human body over six years.
"ARIA, record. Full resolution. Tag it for the payload archive."
"My name is Daniel Kim. I'm thirty-three. I was twenty-seven when they put me on this ship." Daniel's voice was steady despite his weakness. "They gave me the cheapest genetic modifications they had. Basic tier. The minimum."
He paused, gathering strength.
"I'm dying now. My kidneys failed two months ago. My liver is failing. Every system in my body is shutting down because the modifications I received weren't designed to protect me. They were designed to collect data. To see how long a human could survive on Mars with minimal intervention."
Another pause. Cassian saw tears forming in Daniel's damaged eyes.
"I'm not the only one. Everyone who received Basic tier modifications is dead or dying. Sophie Laurent. Rachel Park. Others whose names you'll see in the mission reports. We were test subjects, not crew members. PharmaCorp sorted us into categories—Enhanced, Standard, Basic—and the Basic tier was always meant to die. Our deaths were factored into their projections before we ever launched."
Daniel looked directly at the camera.
"I want you to understand what that means. It means someone in an office on Earth looked at my file and decided I wasn't worth saving. They calculated the cost of better modifications, weighed it against my expected contribution to the mission, and concluded that my life wasn't worth the investment. I was a line item on a spreadsheet. A resource to be optimized."
"And now I'm dying. Slowly. Painfully. While the people who made that decision sit in comfortable chairs and count their profits."
His voice hardened.
"I came to Mars to save lives. I believed in this mission. I believed I was part of something meaningful, something that would make a difference. And maybe I was—maybe the Areserine we produced will save millions of people. But I need you to know the cost. I need you to know that pharmaceutical companies don't care about patients. They care about shareholders. They care about quarterly earnings. And they will let people die—let us die—if it saves them money."
Daniel was crying now, tears streaming down his ravaged face.
"Don't let them do this again. Don't let them send more people to die on Mars without telling them the truth. We were expendable. We were always expendable. And the only thing that makes our deaths mean anything is if you remember us. If you refuse to let them bury our stories with our bodies."
He looked at Cassian, off-camera.
"I'm done. That's all I wanted to say."
"ARIA, end recording." Daniel closed his eyes, exhausted by the effort of speaking truth to a world that might never hear it.
Twelve days later, he was dead.

## The Surgery


### Year 6, Month 8 — August 2042

The pain woke Thomas Wright at 0347 hours.
It started in his lower right abdomen—a sharp, stabbing sensation that made him gasp and curl into a fetal position. He'd felt discomfort for days, dismissed it as stress or bad food or the general malaise that had become everyone's baseline. But this was different. This was wrong.
"ARIA." His voice came out as a croak. "Medical assistance."
`Analyzing symptoms. Please describe the location and nature of your pain.`
"Right side. Lower abdomen. Sharp. Getting worse."
`Thomas Wright, your symptoms are consistent with acute appendicitis. I am alerting Dr. Osei. Please remain still and avoid eating or drinking.`
Amara arrived within minutes, her medical scanner already running. Her expression told Thomas everything he needed to know before she spoke.
"Appendicitis. Probably twelve to eighteen hours from rupture." She met his eyes. "We need to operate, Thomas. I can try to contact Earth for guidance, but the communication delay—"
"Twenty-four minutes round trip." Thomas gritted his teeth against another wave of pain. "How long do I have?"
"If we're lucky, twelve hours. If we're not..." She didn't finish the sentence.
They set up the surgery in the medical bay, converting examination tables into an operating theater. Amara had never performed an appendectomy—she was a physician, not a surgeon. But Dr. Torres was dead, and there was no one else.
"ARIA, connect me to NASA's surgical team. Highest priority."
`Connecting. Estimated response delay: 12 minutes.`
Twelve minutes for every question. Twelve minutes between each step of a procedure that required split-second decisions. Amara had read about operating with communication delays, but the reality was terrifying.
"Okay, Thomas." She forced her voice steady. "We're going to sedate you as much as we can with what we have. It won't be enough to eliminate the pain, but it should make it bearable."
"Do what you have to do."
Cassian assisted, his hands steady despite his complete lack of surgical training. Marcus monitored vitals. Thea managed the communication relay, transcribing Amara's questions and reading Earth's responses with agonizing precision.
"Dr. Osei, this is Dr. Chen at NASA Medical." The response came twelve minutes after Amara's initial message. "We're patching in our surgical team now. Please describe what you're seeing."
Amara described. Twelve minutes later, guidance arrived. She made the incision. Twelve more minutes of waiting while blood welled and Thomas groaned through his sedation.
The procedure took four hours.
Four hours of asking questions she needed answered immediately, waiting twelve minutes for responses, making judgment calls when the guidance didn't quite match what she was seeing. Four hours of Cassian holding retractors and passing instruments, of Marcus calling out vital signs that kept threatening to drop into dangerous territory, of Thomas drifting in and out of consciousness and occasionally screaming when the pain broke through the sedation.
But in the end, the appendix came out. Swollen, inflamed, probably hours from rupture—but intact. Amara closed the incision in careful layers, her hands trembling with exhaustion, and stepped back from the table.
"It's done." Her voice cracked. "He's going to be okay."
Thomas opened his eyes, his face pale and sheened with sweat. "Did you get it?"
"Got it." Amara held up the specimen jar. "Want to keep it as a souvenir?"
"God no." Thomas managed a weak laugh, then winced as the motion pulled at his incision. "How long until I can work?"
"Two weeks minimum."
"We don't have two weeks of margin. One week."
"Thomas—"
"One week, Doc. We can't afford to be down an engineer." He closed his eyes. "Thanks for not killing me."
Amara looked at the specimen jar, at the inflamed organ that had nearly killed a man they couldn't afford to lose. Four hours of surgery with twelve-minute delays. A procedure that would have taken thirty minutes on Earth.
This was what it meant to be 225 million kilometers from help. This was the cost of exploration that no one talked about in the recruitment brochures.
She set down the jar and went to wash the blood from her hands.

The eastern corridor. The viewport at the end of it.
She reached it without having meant to and stopped.
The Martian night was out there — the stars fixed in their positions, the sky she had memorized so thoroughly that any deviation registered before she had consciously processed it.
Three lights that did not belong.
She stared until she was certain.
Ships.
"ARIA."
`Yes, Thea.`
"Three lights. Eastern quadrant, low on the horizon. Tell me what they are."
A silence. Not hesitation — calculation.
`Three vessels on approach. Transponder profiles consistent with Ares IV mission configuration. Current trajectory indicates Mars orbital insertion in twenty-two days.`
`I am attempting contact. Transmitting signal burst on Ares IV approach frequency.`
The habitat was quiet. The life support hummed. Somewhere behind her the organisms pulsed their ancient rhythm, the same as they had always been.
Then the speaker crackled.
The sound was degraded — compressed and stretched by distance and a receiver that had been failing for months — but the cadence was unmistakable. Precise. Authoritative. A voice that had given orders across deep space and expected them followed.
"Ares III mission control, this is Ares IV on approach — do you copy?"
Thea stared at the ships, unresponsive to her disbelief.
"Ares III mission control, this is Ares IV — do you read? Is there anyone down there?"
She stood very still for a moment. Then —
"ARIA. Put me on."
`Channel open.`
She pressed her hand against the viewport. When she spoke her voice was barely recognizable to herself — hoarse and thin, worn years beyond her age, the voice of someone who had not spoken aloud in days.
"Ares IV — this is Thea Miren. Who am I speaking with?"
A silence.
"Well — I'll be damned." A breath. "Thea Miren — this is Commander Mason. We haven't received a single transmission from you in months. We thought — hell, we thought you were all dead."
Thea closed her eyes for a moment. When she opened them the three lights were still there, still moving, still twenty-two days away.
"We are, Commander." A pause. "We are dead. All of us."
A silence.
"I'm sorry — did you say that you're all dead?"
"ARIA — send Commander Mason a full status report."
`Transmitted.`
She waited. The lights moved imperceptibly against the fixed stars. The life support cycled. She did not sit down. She did not know if she would be able to get back up.
A silence — longer this time.
"Thea — I've reviewed the status report." A pause — and something in the quality of Mason's silence told her he was choosing his next words carefully, the way people do when they understand they are speaking into a moment they cannot fix. "I need you to hear something. Your payload arrived in full. Every kilogram. Hundreds of thousands of patients treated in the first year alone."
A silence.
"It worked, Thea."
Thea said nothing.
The sound that came out of her was not a word. It was the sound a person makes when something they have been bracing against for years simply — stops. An exhale that was almost a sob and not quite either. Her free hand came up to the glass. Her eyes blurred.
She reached into her sweatshirt pocket and slowly drew out the photograph. She held it up toward the faint light from the viewport and looked at them. And there was Cassian, among the few others, looking to the camera, as though he were looking at her in this very moment. Her eyes, bloodshot and teared up, moved from face to face.
She barely managed more than a shallow smile, as a single tear found its way down her cheek.
She pressed her forehead briefly against the back of her hand. Her shoulders moved once.
Then she whispered — not into the channel, not for Mason, not for ARIA or the record or anyone living —
"Cassian. You did it."
A breath.
"Magnificent."
She closed her eyes and tucked the picture back into her pocket.

---

Outside, three lights that were not stars continued their slow and patient approach.
Thea exhaled and slowly sank to her knees, dragging her hand along the glass until it met her side.
Fragmented memories flooded her mind. She thought about her father's face looking up from his desk when she came in, the way it changed when he saw her, the way she had never once walked into that room and not been wanted there.
She thought about her mother crying at the mathematics prize and failing to hide it.
She thought about being very small, her mother kneeling beside her, holding their dying dog gently in her arms, explaining quietly, for the first time, that some subtractions in life are permanent.
She remembered launch day, helmet in hand, the celebratory noise, waving to the cameras.
Finding Cassian's eyes between it all, for a fleeting moment — a connection that had no name yet. Something in the way he'd looked at her as they entered the ship. Not at the crew. At her.
"Thea — are you there, copy?"
His words landed displaced, as all sound faded into silence.
Thea lowered her head until her cheek rested against the cold surface of the viewport.
She would not lift it again.

## Earth


### PharmaCorp Payload Processing Facility — Cambridgeshire

August 2, 2043

Technician Aldridge had been on the payload retrieval team for nine days.

The work was methodical — each item logged against the manifest, photographed, transferred to the appropriate processing team. He had done this kind of work before on unmanned return missions and knew its rhythms. You moved carefully and you checked twice and you flagged anything that didn't match.

On the ninth day he found something that didn't match.

It was in the geological equipment casing — a survey tool he'd logged three identical units of already. This one was eleven grams heavier than its listed weight. The serial number referenced a PharmaCorp equipment registry code that didn't exist in any system he had access to.

He held it for a moment.

Then he logged the casing as processed, tucked the chip into the inner side of his boot, and finished his shift.

That evening he reviewed its contents on his personal laptop, in his car, in the facility car park with the engine running.

Elena Vasquez's testimony was the first file. The same forty-one minutes — her flat precise voice, her field report register, everything she'd witnessed stated without attachment or reference. He listened to the end.

Then he opened the second partition.

The documents were organized the way a scientist organizes evidence — chronologically, cross-referenced, indexed. He was not a scientist and he was not a lawyer but he had been working payload retrieval for eleven years and he understood what he was looking at. He understood what it meant that these files existed and what it meant that they were here, hidden in a geological equipment casing with a false serial number, rather than anywhere PharmaCorp could have found them first.

He sat in the car park for a long time.

Then he drove home, and the following morning he uploaded everything — testimony and evidence together, indexed exactly as Elena Vasquez had organized it — across seventeen separate channels simultaneously. No covering message. No attribution.

He went back to work.

The files were already everywhere.

### PharmaCorp Strategic Planning — London

August 23, 2043

Forsythe read it on his phone at the kitchen table before his daughter came down for breakfast.

He'd watched the testimony files three weeks ago — eighteen recordings, processed in order, door closed, most of them received with the professional distance of a man who had spent a career in long-duration mission planning and knew how to keep that kind of material from pooling somewhere it would cause him problems later. Elena Vasquez had been the exception. Forty-one minutes in the flat precise register of a field report, and he'd found he could not look away — not because of the accusations, but because of the quality of attention she brought to making them. The way a scientist sounds when they are stating what they know for the last time and they know it. He had not taken notes on her recording. He had noted afterward that he was still thinking about it, which seemed like the kind of thing a person ought to do.

He had also noted that it hadn't changed anything in the Ares IV planning document.

Now the files were everywhere. Indexed, cross-referenced, organized with the precision of someone who had known exactly who would eventually find them and wanted to make it as easy as possible to understand. Elena Vasquez's testimony was the first file. The documents were the second. Together they were complete in a way that forty-one minutes of careful testimony alone had never been.

He set his phone face-down on the table.

His daughter came downstairs. She wanted to know if he was coming to her recital on Friday. He said yes. She poured herself cereal and talked about something that had happened at school, and he listened, and when she left for the bus he sat alone at the table for a while.

Then he went to his office and opened the Ares IV planning document.

The notation was there. Six weeks of contingency rather than four. The humanitarian language in the committee memo. The accelerated training schedule citing Ares III precedent.

He read it through once.

He closed the document without changing anything.

### October 2043 — Houston, Texas

The waiting room had a television mounted in the corner, and the television was showing Cassian Vale.
She'd seen the footage before — everyone had by now, four months after the payload landed — but she watched it again because there was nothing else to do and because the nurse had said twenty minutes and it had been thirty-five. The man on the screen looked terrible. Radiation burns across his jaw and neck. Eyes that had the fixed quality of someone managing pain through concentration. He was reading from something, or maybe not reading — maybe he'd memorized it, or maybe he'd said it enough times in practice that it came out that way, measured and certain, like something he'd decided on rather than something he'd written.
This is my testimony. My name is Cassian Vale.
She'd had Stage 2 when they told her. The Areserine distribution center was three bus transfers and a four-month wait. She'd done the transfers. She'd done the wait.
The nurse appeared in the doorway and called her name.
She got up. The television kept going — Cassian Vale, still talking, still looking directly at the lens with the fixed attention of someone who knows exactly who he's addressing and that they won't hear it for months. She didn't hear the rest of it. She followed the nurse down the corridor and into the treatment room and sat in the chair they pointed to, and a technician she'd never met hung a bag on a hook and connected it to the line in her arm, and the first treatment began.
Outside, in the waiting room, a dozen other people sat beneath the television. Some of them watched. Some of them looked at their phones. One of them was crying quietly, and no one said anything about it because they all understood.

PharmaCorp was never criminally prosecuted.
The evidence was damning, but the legal frameworks for holding corporations accountable for deaths on Mars didn't exist. Treaties were unclear. Jurisdiction was contested. PharmaCorp's army of lawyers found enough procedural obstacles to delay any trial indefinitely. Civil settlements were reached — confidential amounts, sealed agreements, enough money to silence most families who couldn't afford years of litigation.
Elena Vasquez's documents reached Earth in the payload archive — internal planning emails, the genetic tier cost spreadsheets, Research Protocol 7-C with its subject population specified in section 1.2. Investigative journalists published them. PharmaCorp disputed chain of custody and data integrity. Civil suits were filed, settled under confidentiality agreements, the amounts never disclosed. The REE extraction plans entered the congressional record. No legislation followed.
The company issued a statement:
"PharmaCorp is deeply saddened by the loss of our Ares III crew members. Their heroic sacrifice will never be forgotten. We have implemented enhanced protocols for future missions to ensure that lessons learned from Ares III inform our ongoing commitment to crew safety and mission success. Science for Humanity."
Behind closed doors, planning continued for Ares IV.
